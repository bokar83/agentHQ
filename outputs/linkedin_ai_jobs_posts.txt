POST 1: THE WRONG QUESTION

'Will AI take my job?'

Wrong question.

The right question: 'Am I building skills AI can't replicate?'

Here's what I see in consulting work:

AI automates tasks. It doesn't automate judgment.
AI generates options. It doesn't choose which one matters.
AI processes data. It doesn't understand what the data means for YOUR specific situation.

The people losing ground aren't losing to AI.
They're losing to people who use AI.

There's a difference.

If your entire value proposition is 'I do tasks efficiently,' you're in trouble. You were already in trouble before ChatGPT launched.

But if your value is:
- Understanding messy human problems
- Making calls when there's no clear answer
- Building trust in high-stakes situations
- Asking the questions no one else is asking

You're not competing with AI. You're using it.

The displacement is real. But it's not new. Every technology shift does this.

The question isn't whether AI will take jobs.
It's whether you're building the skills that matter when AI handles everything else.

POST 2: THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH

Everyone wants to talk about AI ethics and job displacement.

No one wants to talk about who's actually at risk.

Let me be direct:

If you're in a role where 'following the process' is your main contribution, AI is coming for that. Fast.

If you're in a role where 'knowing the answer' is your expertise, AI already knows more than you.

But here's what AI can't do:

It can't sit across from a CEO who's about to make a decision that could save or sink their company and read the room.

It can't navigate the politics of getting a good idea approved in a broken organization.

It can't take a vague problem, ask the right questions, and figure out what actually needs solving.

These aren't 'soft skills.' They're the HARD skills. The ones that matter when everything else is automated.

The real question isn't 'Will AI take jobs?'

It's 'What are you doing with the time AI is giving you back?'

Are you using it to do more of the same?
Or are you using it to do the work that actually moves the needle?

Because the people who figure that out aren't worried about AI taking their jobs.

They're worried about their competition figuring it out first.

POST 3: WHAT THEY DON'T TELL YOU ABOUT AI AND JOBS

The AI job displacement conversation is missing something critical.

It's not about whether AI will take jobs. It will.
It's about WHICH jobs. And why.

Picture this scenario:

Two analysts. Same company. Same role.

Analyst A uses AI to do their current work faster. More reports. More slides. Same thinking.

Analyst B uses AI to eliminate the routine work entirely. Then uses that time to understand the business, build relationships, ask better questions.

In 18 months, one of them is worried about AI. The other is running strategy.

The difference isn't the tool. It's the mindset.

Here's the pattern I keep seeing:

AI doesn't replace people who solve problems.
It replaces people who execute solutions to problems someone else defined.

If your job is to 'run the numbers' – AI will run them faster.
If your job is to figure out which numbers actually matter – you're irreplaceable.

If your job is to 'write the code' – AI will write it cheaper.
If your job is to architect systems that solve real business problems – you're more valuable than ever.

The displacement is real. But it's not random.

It's exposing what was always true:

Tasks are automatable.
Judgment isn't.

Process is replicable.
Wisdom isn't.

The question isn't whether AI will take your job.
It's whether you're doing work that deserves to exist when AI handles everything else.

And if the answer makes you uncomfortable, good.

That discomfort is the signal to start building different skills.
