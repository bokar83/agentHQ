# LinkedIn Posts: Will AI Take People's Jobs?

## POST 1: The Wrong Question

Everyone's asking "Will AI take my job?"

Wrong question.

Last month, I watched a $2B manufacturing client cut 40% of their data entry team. Not because of AI. Because they finally automated what should have been automated in 2015.

Here's what I'm seeing in the field:

→ AI isn't taking jobs. Bad processes wrapped in human labor are getting exposed.
→ The people who lose out? Those doing work that should never have been manual in the first place.
→ The people who win? Those who can think, synthesize, and make judgment calls AI can't touch.

I spent yesterday with a client's strategy team. Half their time goes to pulling data from 6 different systems into PowerPoint. That's not strategic work. That's digital janitorial work dressed up as analysis.

AI won't take jobs. It'll force us to be honest about what's actually valuable work.

The uncomfortable truth: If your job can be fully automated by current AI, it probably should have been automated years ago.

The opportunity: Stop defending indefensible work. Start building skills AI can't replicate - judgment, synthesis, stakeholder navigation, creative problem-solving.

What work are you doing that AI genuinely can't? That's your moat.

## POST 2: The Consulting Reality Check

Hot take from the trenches:

AI isn't replacing consultants. It's exposing the ones who were always just PowerPoint decorators.

Three weeks ago, a Big 4 partner told me their junior analysts now produce in 2 days what used to take 2 weeks. His conclusion? "We need fewer analysts."

He's wrong. And here's why this matters for everyone, not just consultants:

We don't need fewer people. We need different people doing different work.

The analysts who spent 80% of their time formatting slides and pulling data? Yeah, that's toast. But the ones who could talk to a CFO, understand the real problem behind the stated problem, and craft a recommendation that actually gets implemented? They're more valuable than ever.

Here's the pattern I'm seeing across industries:

• AI handles the "what" → data, patterns, initial analysis
• Humans own the "so what" → context, implications, decisions
• The gap between them? That's where the real work lives now

Last quarter, I worked with a retail client using AI for demand forecasting. The AI was 15% more accurate than their old models. Know what made the difference between success and failure? A 20-year merchant who knew how to interpret those forecasts through the lens of vendor relationships, supply chain constraints, and store manager behavior.

The AI gave better numbers. The human made them useful.

Your job isn't safe because AI can't do it. Your job is safe because you can do what matters AFTER the AI does its part.

Different game. Different skills. Same urgency.

## POST 3: What Nobody's Saying About AI and Jobs

The AI job apocalypse isn't coming.

It's already here. You're just looking at the wrong thing.

Two months ago, I sat in a boardroom where executives debated cutting 200 customer service jobs because of AI. Three hours of hand-wringing about ethics, optics, and transition plans.

Then I asked: "How many of those 200 people are empowered to actually solve customer problems vs. reading from scripts?"

Silence.

The truth nobody wants to say out loud:

Most "jobs" lost to AI were never really jobs. They were roles we created because we couldn't automate yet, not because the work required human capability.

I'm not being callous. I'm being honest.

Here's what I see in real companies, right now:

✗ AI isn't eliminating jobs that require judgment
✗ AI isn't eliminating jobs that require relationship building
✗ AI isn't eliminating jobs that require navigating ambiguity

✓ AI IS eliminating jobs that are basically human API calls
✓ AI IS eliminating jobs that follow rigid scripts
✓ AI IS eliminating jobs that transform data from format A to format B

The uncomfortable question: If your entire job can be captured in a detailed SOP document, why does it need to be a job?

But here's the opportunity everyone misses:

Every company I work with is desperate for people who can:
→ Translate between technical and business
→ Design processes that actually work (not just look good on paper)
→ Navigate organizational politics to get things done
→ Ask the right questions, not just find the right answers

These jobs don't have formal titles yet. They're emerging in the gap between "what AI can do" and "what organizations need."

The real question isn't "Will AI take jobs?"

It's "Are you building skills for work that actually needs humans, or are you defending work that's already obsolete?"

Harsh? Maybe.
True? Definitely.
Actionable? Your move.
