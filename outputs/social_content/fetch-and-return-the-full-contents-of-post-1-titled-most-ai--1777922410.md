VARIATION 1:

Vulnerability: The scariest thing I've learned as a leader.

I spent years hiding my uncertainty. Thought it made me look competent. It didn't.

Last quarter, a client's AI implementation stalled. My team knew the approach was flawed but stayed silent. I finally said: "I don't think this will work. Let's start over."

The room went quiet. Then someone spoke up. Then another. Within an hour, we had a better plan.

That moment taught me: vulnerability isn't weakness. It's permission for your team to do the same.

When I facilitate AI strategy sessions now, I lead with what I don't know. "Here's what I'm uncertain about." "I need your perspective on this." The conversations that follow are where the actual work happens.

Leadership isn't about having all the answers. It's about creating space for the right questions.

Try it next week. Share one thing you're unsure about in your next meeting. Watch what happens.

VARIATION 2:

Vulnerability: The scariest thing I've learned as a leader.

The fear of looking weak kept me from embracing this for too long. I thought vulnerability meant oversharing. It doesn't.

It means admitting when you're wrong. When a strategy isn't working. When you need help.

I remember a board meeting where I had to say: "This isn't working as I'd hoped. What am I missing?"

The room shifted. People leaned in. We found the blind spot I couldn't see.

That's the pattern I've seen across organizations: leaders who admit uncertainty get better input, faster decisions, and stronger outcomes. Not because they're less capable, but because they're more honest.

In my work with AI adoption, I create spaces where risks can be discussed without judgment. Where "I don't know" is a valid answer.

It's still scary. But that's where the growth happens.

Start small. Acknowledge one limitation publicly. Watch how it transforms your dynamics.
