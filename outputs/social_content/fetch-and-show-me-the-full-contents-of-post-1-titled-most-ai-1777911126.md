Vulnerability: The scariest thing I've learned as a leader.

I spent the first five years of my career trying to look like I had it all figured out. Then I lost a key client because I wouldn't admit I'd misread their needs. That cost me more than the contract.

Here's what I learned: when you're wrong, say it. Not "my bad" or "I'll look into it." Say "I made a mistake. Here's what happened. Here's how we fix it."

Last quarter, our product launch was two weeks out and we discovered a critical bug. I could have buried it. Instead, I told the team: "This is on me. I approved the timeline without enough testing. We're shifting priorities."

The room went quiet. Then someone said, "Thanks for telling us." That's when I realized vulnerability isn't about weakness, it's about giving people permission to do the same.

I still get nervous before I admit mistakes. But the trust I've built since? Worth every uncomfortable conversation.

VARIATION 2:
Vulnerability: The scariest thing I've learned as a leader.

I used to think leaders were supposed to have all the answers. Then I realized: nobody does. Not really.

Three years ago, I was leading a team through a merger. Everyone was anxious. I kept saying "trust the process" while I had no idea what the process actually was. The team noticed. Morale tanked.

I called a meeting and said: "I don't have this figured out either. But I do know we're in it together. What are you worried about?"

That's when the real conversation started. People shared concerns I'd never heard. We solved problems I wouldn't have found otherwise.

Vulnerability isn't about having no confidence. It's about having enough to admit you don't know everything. It's about creating space for your team to bring their full selves to work.

I still catch myself wanting to pretend. But the cost of pretending is always higher.

What's one thing you've learned by being honest with your team?
