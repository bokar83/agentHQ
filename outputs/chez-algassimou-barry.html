Where Family Recipes Meet Atlanta

Algassimou Barry grew up in Conakry, Guinea, where food brought everyone together—neighbors, cousins, friends who became family. His mother cooked over charcoal fires, grinding her own spices, slow-simmering stews that filled the compound with aromas you could taste in the air.

When he moved to Atlanta in 2008, he couldn't find that same food anywhere. The warmth. The depth. The way a proper mafé makes you close your eyes on the first bite. So in 2019, he opened Chez Algassimou Barry in a modest space on Buford Highway, cooking the dishes he grew up with, exactly as his mother taught him.

Today, the restaurant serves Guinean and Senegalese classics to a mix of West African expats hungry for home and Atlanta diners discovering these flavors for the first time. The jollof rice debate happens here weekly. The fufu is pounded fresh. And if Algassimou is in the kitchen when you visit, he'll probably come out to make sure you're eating well.

What We Cook

Every dish starts with whole spices, fresh vegetables, and time. We don't rush the process.

Poulet Yassa

Grilled chicken marinated overnight in lemon, mustard, and caramelized onions. Served with jasmine rice or attieke (fermented cassava couscous). The marinade is tangy, savory, and slightly sweet—a Senegalese standard done right.

Mafé

Slow-cooked peanut stew with your choice of lamb, beef, or vegetables. Rich, earthy, with a hint of heat from Scotch bonnet peppers. Served over white rice with a side of steamed cabbage.

Thieboudienne (Thieb)

Senegal's national dish. Fish stewed with tomatoes, vegetables, and broken rice cooked in the same pot until every grain absorbs the sauce. It's messy, communal, and worth the wait.

Fouti Sauce

Okra stew cooked down until silky, with smoked fish, palm oil, and greens. Served with fufu (pounded cassava) or white rice. Not for the timid—this one has depth.

Grilled Tilapia

Whole tilapia rubbed with ginger, garlic, and pepper, grilled over charcoal. Comes with attiéké and a side of spicy tomato-onion relish.

Jollof Rice

West Africa's most debated dish. Ours is Guinean-style: tomato-forward, slightly smoky, with a crispy bottom layer (socarrat) if you ask nicely.

Aloco (Fried Plantains)

Ripe plantains fried until caramelized, served with a spicy pepper sauce for dipping.

Bissap (Hibiscus Drink)

Sweet-tart hibiscus tea served cold. Refreshing, floral, and the perfect balance to rich, savory dishes.

Ginger Juice

Fresh ginger blended with pineapple and a touch of lime. Bright, spicy, and made to order.
