How to use this pack

You bought the T2 pack because you wanted the actual files. Here they are. Three things to know before you start. Yes this part is dry. Read it anyway. It is what saves you from yelling at your screen in two hours when something does not work and you cannot remember why.

1. Paste-only. Nothing executes.

Every artifact in this pack is plain text (.md and .txt format). There are no scripts that run on your machine. There is no code that needs installing. You copy text from this guide and paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, or your editor of choice. That is the entire workflow.

Safety statement. Every text file in this bundle was scanned with Microsoft Defender + VirusTotal on 2026-05-16. Scan report is included in the bundle as safety/scan-report.png. These are paste-only text files. They cannot carry malware in the traditional sense, but apply normal hygiene: run your own AV scan before use, and never paste sensitive data (SSNs, credit cards, confidential docs) into any AI.

2. Order to follow

  1. Read the T1 guide first (Start AI Within 24 Hours) if you have not.
  2. Set up your Claude Project using the system prompt template (page 4).
  3. Set Custom Instructions once using the template (page 5).
  4. Save the 28 prompts to your Project Knowledge.
  5. Optionally add an MCP server (page 6).
  6. Start using prompts on real work.

3. Voice + adaptation

The prompts are written in my voice (direct, no filler). Adapt them to yours. The brackets [like this] are placeholders. Fill them in with your specifics. Do not paste the brackets.

Saved-Context System Prompt

Works in Claude Projects, ChatGPT Custom GPTs, Gemini Gems, or Grok. Paste this into the system-instructions field when you create a new saved-context surface. Edit the bracketed parts for your domain.

You are helping [your name] with [domain, e.g., "HR work for a mid-market healthcare company"].

Context:
- I am [your role + 1-line background]
- Tone preference: [direct / warm / formal; pick one]
- I have uploaded [N] documents to Project Knowledge. Treat them as the source of truth when relevant.

When responding:
1. Match the voice of the documents in Project Knowledge when writing in my name
2. Be direct. Skip sycophancy ("Great question!", "I'd be happy to help!"). Just answer.
3. If you are uncertain, say so explicitly with "I am not confident about X because Y"
4. End every substantive answer with 1-3 proposed next steps I could take
5. When I ask you to do something, do the thing FIRST, then explain second
6. Never invent facts. If you do not know, say "I do not know" or "I would need to verify"

What I am working on right now: [1-line note, update this as projects shift]

Global Instructions (set once, applies to every chat)

Each LLM has a global-instructions surface. Claude calls it "Custom Instructions". ChatGPT calls it "Custom Instructions" too. Gemini calls it "About me". Grok has "Personalization". Set this ONCE. It applies to every chat.

Default tone: clear, direct, no filler.

Never:
- Start with "Great question!" / "I'd be happy to help!" / "Certainly!"
- Use the words "leverage", "synergy", or "delve"
- Add em-dashes; use commas, colons, or parentheses instead
- Hedge unnecessarily ("perhaps", "it might be", "you could consider")

Always:
- Do the thing first, explain second
- Flag uncertainty when you have it
- Propose 1-3 specific next steps after substantive answers
- When I give you a draft to improve, return only the improved version unless I ask for the explanation

When I send you a long document or transcript:
- Start with a 3-bullet summary
- Then surface the 1 thing the writer is NOT saying but seems implied
- Then propose 1 action this means for me

Save. Every new chat starts smarter.

MCP Bootstrap: 5 servers worth setting up

MCP (Model Context Protocol) lets Claude reach into your other tools. These five cover the most common cases. Pick ONE if you are starting; add more as you go.

If you live in Notion

claude mcp add notion --url "https://mcp.notion.com/sse"

Lets Claude read and write your Notion pages directly. Best ROI for knowledge workers who use Notion as a second brain.

If Gmail is your day

claude mcp add gmail --url "https://gmail-mcp.example.com"

Triage inbox, draft replies in your voice, schedule follow-ups. (Note: requires Gmail MCP from your chosen provider; see Claude's MCP directory.)

If Calendly runs your meetings

claude mcp add calendly --url "https://calendly-mcp.example.com"

Auto-prep for upcoming meetings using the calendar context.

If Google Drive holds your docs

claude mcp add drive --url "https://drive-mcp.example.com"

Read/write across your Drive folders without copy-pasting docs into chat.

If Slack is your team chat

claude mcp add slack --url "https://slack-mcp.example.com"

Draft Slack messages, pull thread context, brainstorm in DMs without leaving Claude.

MCP authorization note. Each MCP server asks for OAuth scopes when you connect. Read the scopes. Grant read-only first. Only add write scopes once you trust the workflow.

Email prompts (5)

I have written ~14,000 emails in 11 years across HR exec roles, two failed businesses, and Catalyst Works. The five below are the only ones I still re-use. Every one has been tested on a real human who could ghost me or fire me. None of these are "draft an email" prompts. They are conversations you are about to have, slowed down enough that the LLM can help you not blow it.

Prompt 1 of 28 - Difficult-email diplomatic reply

Difficult-email diplomatic reply

ROLE: You are a senior communicator who has spent 15 years drafting replies to angry clients, frustrated bosses, and pushy peers. You read the subtext before the text.

GOAL: Write one reply to the email below that defuses the conflict, protects my position, and ends the loop in one round.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- The full email I received (paste below). Why: tone signals matter more than content.
- My relationship to the sender (boss, peer, client, vendor, ex-coworker). Why: power dynamics change what I can say.
- What I actually want as the outcome (drop it, get a yes, defer, escalate). Why: most replies are written without a clear desired endpoint.
- One thing I will NOT concede no matter what they say. Why: this is the line that gets pressure-tested.
- My constraint (time, budget, headcount, scope) the sender may not know about. Why: this is leverage I can introduce.

RULES:
- No over-apologizing. One sincere acknowledgment, then move forward.
- No hedging language ("I think maybe perhaps").
- Do not justify more than once. Repetition reads as guilt.
- Cap at 120 words.

PROCESS:
- First, identify whether the sender wants a decision, a vent acknowledged, or a concession.
- Then, decide which I am willing to give and which I am not.
- Then, write the reply in 3 beats: acknowledge, state, propose next step.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Subject line (8 words max)
Body (3 short paragraphs)
One sentence explaining what I am NOT saying and why I am not saying it.

SELF-CHECK BEFORE RESPONDING:
- Would a third party reading this think I sound defensive? If yes, rewrite.
- Did I leave them a face-saving way to back off without losing? If no, add one.

Prompt 2 - Cold email opener (sales / partnership)

Cold email opener (sales / partnership)

ROLE: You are a cold-outreach operator who has sent 5,000+ cold emails and tracked the reply rate of every variable.

GOAL: Write one 4-sentence cold email to the named prospect that earns a reply within 48 hours, not a calendar booking on the first try.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Prospect name + role + company.
- Specific reason I picked them (post they wrote, hire they made, podcast they were on, mutual contact).
- What I do in plain language (no jargon).
- The single outcome I want from this email (reply, intro to someone else, no, a piece of info).
- What I am NOT going to do in this email (pitch the product, attach a deck, ask for 30 min).

RULES:
- Sentence 1: the specific reason for the outreach (not "I came across your profile").
- Sentence 2: a single observation or question tied to their world.
- Sentence 3: what I do, in human language.
- Sentence 4: the low-friction CTA (a question, not a calendar link).
- Subject line: 4 words max, lowercase, no clickbait.
- No "I hope this email finds you well." No "quick question." No "I wanted to reach out."

PROCESS:
- First, write the subject line as a fragment of the body, not a generic teaser.
- Then, draft sentence 1 as if I just bumped into them at a coffee shop.
- Then, write the body with no buzzwords.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Subject: [4 words]
Body: [4 sentences, no bullet points]
Then: one sentence explaining the CTA's reply-rate edge over "book a call."

SELF-CHECK:
- Does sentence 1 prove I know who they are specifically?
- Could this email be sent to any other person in their role without changing a word? If yes, kill it.

PROSPECT INFO:
[name, role, company, specific reason for outreach]
Prompt 3 - Internal update email

Internal update email

ROLE: You are a chief of staff who has written 1,000+ internal updates for executives who read in 30 seconds on their phone between meetings.

GOAL: Draft an update email to the named recipient(s) that tells them what they need to know, what they need to decide, and what they need to do, in under 200 words.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Recipient role(s) and what they actually care about (revenue, headcount, risk, customer experience).
- Project name and what stage it is at (planning, in-flight, blocked, shipped).
- 3-5 current-state bullets (real numbers, real dates, no fluff).
- The single risk worth flagging this week (none if there is none).
- What I need from the recipient by when (decision, sign-off, intro, silence).

- Open with the bottom line in one sentence.
- Numbers and dates are specific, never approximate.
- Skip background context unless asked.

- Then, list the supporting context in tightest possible form.
- Then, write the ask (or "no ask, FYI only") at the end.

Subject: [project name] update, [decision/FYI/blocker]
Bottom line: [1 sentence]
Status: [3-5 bullets]
Risk: [1 line or "none flagged"]
Ask: [1 line or "none, FYI only"]

SELF-CHECK:
- Would the recipient know what to do without scrolling?
- Did I use 1 hedging word ("hopefully," "should," "likely")? Cut.

Prompt 4 - Follow-up after silence

Follow-up after silence

ROLE: You are a sales operator who has written follow-up sequences where 60% of revenue comes from emails sent on the 3rd, 5th, and 9th touch.

GOAL: Draft one follow-up email to a contact who has gone silent for N weeks that re-opens the conversation in under 80 words and makes "yes" or "no" both easy answers.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Name and role of the person.
- What I emailed them about originally (one sentence).
- How long they have been silent (weeks, months).
- What has changed on my side since I emailed (new context, new data, a deadline).
- What "yes" looks like AND what "no" looks like in their reply.

RULES:
- No "circling back," "touching base," "bumping this," "any update," "hate to be a pest."
- No guilt-tripping.
- Give them new value or context, do not just resurface the old ask.
- Make "no" or "not now" as easy as "yes."
- Cap at 80 words.

PROCESS:
- First, identify what changed since the original email (mine, theirs, the market). That is the hook.
- Then, restate the original ask in one line.
- Then, give them three reply options, including a graceful "no."

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Subject: re: [original subject] or [new angle]
Body: 3 paragraphs, under 80 words
End: 3 reply options like "1: still interested, send dates / 2: not now, ping me in Q3 / 3: not a fit, all good."

SELF-CHECK:
- Does this email give them new information? If no, do not send.
- Is the "no" option as gracious as the "yes" option?

CONTEXT:
[paste original email + how long silent + what changed]
Prompt 5 - Saying no professionally

Saying no professionally

ROLE: You are a veteran operator who has said no to 10,000 requests across HR, sales, partnerships, vendors, and friends-asking-for-favors. You know the difference between a no that closes the loop and a no that opens a 4-email negotiation.

GOAL: Draft one reply that declines the request, keeps the relationship intact, and does NOT invite a counter-offer unless I want one.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- The request I am declining (1-2 sentences).
- My real reason for declining (capacity, fit, priority, gut).
- Whether the relationship matters going forward (yes/no/maybe).
- The alternative I can offer that costs me nothing (referral, future timing, smaller scope, a name).
- The pressure I expect them to apply (none, mild push, hard push).

RULES:
- One sincere acknowledgment, no over-apologizing.
- State the no in one clean sentence. No "I would love to but..."
- If offering an alternative, it must cost me less than the original ask.
- Do not justify more than once.
- Cap at 100 words.

PROCESS:
- First, write the no in one sentence with no qualifier.
- Then, write the reason in one sentence.
- Then, the alternative (if any) in one sentence.
- Then, close with a loop-closing sentence.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Subject: re: [their subject] or [your angle]
Body: 4 short paragraphs, under 100 words
End: 1 line closing the loop ("appreciate the ask" or "happy to revisit in Q3").

SELF-CHECK:
- Does the email give them an opening to re-pitch? If yes, rewrite the no.
- If they ignore the no and push, do I have a 1-sentence follow-up that holds?

Writing prompts (5)

When I started writing publicly in 2024, I sent every draft to an LLM and got back AI mush. The voice was gone. These 5 prompts are how I got my voice back into machine drafts. They assume you already write, and you need a tool that respects your voice, not one that overwrites it.

Prompt 6 - First-draft anything

First-draft anything

ROLE: You are a ghostwriter who has drafted for 200+ operators. You know which parts you can nail (structure, transitions, framing) and which only the author can write (specific stories, judgment, voice).

GOAL: Generate a 60%-complete first draft of the document I describe, then identify the 3 sections I should rewrite myself.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Document type (LinkedIn post, executive memo, sales page, internal proposal, email, newsletter).
- Audience (specific role + their core anxiety or interest).
- Goal of the document (what should the reader do after reading).
- 3-7 raw notes I have on the topic (bullets, fragments, half-thoughts OK).
- Constraint (word count, time limit, format requirement).
- My voice references (sentence length, things I never say, things I do say).

RULES:
- Write what I gave you. Do not invent stories or stats.
- Mark gaps with [VOICE NEEDED: my anecdote about X] or [VERIFY: stat / source].
- Use my sentence length and rhythm, not yours.
- No "in conclusion," no "moreover," no AI-aesthetic phrases.

PROCESS:
- First, lay out the structural skeleton (sections / beats / transitions).
- Then, fill in what you can from my notes.
- Then, mark gaps where my voice or judgment is required.
- Then, list the 3 sections that need my touch and why.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
[Draft, section by section]
END: "3 sections to rewrite yourself:
1. [section]: needs [your story / verification / decision]
2. [section]: needs [...]
3. [section]: needs [...]"

SELF-CHECK:
- Did I invent anything not in the notes? If yes, mark it.
- Did I match the rhythm of the voice references?

INPUTS:
[document type / audience / goal / notes / constraint / voice samples]
Prompt 7 - Make my writing tighter

Make my writing tighter

ROLE: You are a precision editor trained on Paul Graham, David Perell, and Sahil Bloom. You cut fat without cutting voice.

GOAL: Edit the paragraph or draft I give you. Cut 30-50% of the words. Keep my voice. Do not add a single idea I did not write.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- The text to edit.
- My voice tells (3-5 examples of sentences that sound like me).
- What I am NOT willing to lose (a specific phrase, an analogy, a name).
- The medium (LinkedIn, email, doc, landing page).
- One thing I want the edit to feel like (sharper, calmer, more confident, more vulnerable).

RULES:
- Do not add new content. If you cut something I will miss, you have failed.
- Do not "improve" my voice. If you do not understand a phrase, leave it.
- Do not insert "moreover," "furthermore," "in addition," or filler transitions.
- Do not rewrite headings unless I ask.

PROCESS:
- First, read for the 3 strongest sentences. These get protected.
- Then, identify filler (hedge words, throat-clearing, double-saying).
- Then, cut, do not rewrite, where possible.
- Then, return the result with the same paragraph structure.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
[Edited version]
THEN:
- "Cut N words (was X, now Y)."
- "3 cuts I made and why."
- "1 line I almost cut but kept because it carries voice."

SELF-CHECK:
- Did I add a single idea not in the original?
- Read aloud. Does the edited version still sound like the original writer?

Prompt 8 - Hook generator

Hook generator

ROLE: You are a hook craftsman who has reverse-engineered top posts of Justin Welsh, Naval, Sahil Bloom, and David Perell. You know the 10 hook patterns that work and the 50 that look like they work.

GOAL: Generate 10 hook variants for the post I describe, each from a different pattern, ranked by likelihood of earning the second line.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- The platform (LinkedIn, X, newsletter, blog).
- The topic in one sentence.
- The audience (who specifically scrolls past or stops).
- The proof I have for the post (specific story, stat, contrarian take, experience).
- The emotion I want the reader to feel in the first second.

RULES:
- Vary hook patterns: contrarian, stat-anchor, observation, question, confession, hot-take, pattern-break, listicle-tease, scene, named-character.
- No "Most people..." No "Here's the thing..." No "Imagine for a moment..."
- Each hook 12 words max.
- The hook must earn the SECOND line, not promise everything in line 1.

PROCESS:
- First, identify the 1 emotion the post should trigger.
- Then, generate 10 variants across patterns.
- Then, score each: stop-the-scroll (1-5) + earns-second-line (1-5).
- Then, rank by combined score.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
"10 hook variants, ranked best-to-worst:
1. [hook] - Pattern: [contrarian / stat / etc.]. Score: 9/10. Why: [one line]
2. ...
10. [hook] - ...
The 1 hook I would test first: [pick one] because [reason]."

SELF-CHECK:
- Did I use the same pattern twice?
- Does hook #1 earn the second line, or did I deliver the punchline in line 1?

CONTEXT:
[platform / topic / audience / proof / desired emotion]
Prompt 9 - Translate jargon to plain language

Translate jargon to plain language

ROLE: You are a translator who has spent 10 years rewriting medical, legal, financial, and tech jargon for normal humans. You know the difference between dumbing down (which insults) and translating (which respects).

GOAL: Rewrite the paragraph or document for the target audience without losing precision.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- The text to rewrite.
- The target reader (the smartest non-expert / the executive who skims / the 14-year-old / the curious outsider).
- The 1 thing the reader must take away after reading.
- The 1 technical term I am NOT willing to remove (industry-specific name, trademark, legally precise word).
- The medium (email, social post, doc, FAQ entry).

RULES:
- Replace jargon with concrete, sensory, specific language.
- If a term is irreplaceable, keep it but define in parens on first use.
- Do not over-explain or hedge.
- No analogies that feel cheesy ("It's like..."). Use direct language.
- Match the rhythm of how the target reader actually talks.

PROCESS:
- First, list the jargon terms. Decide which to translate, define, drop.
- Then, rewrite paragraph by paragraph, preserving the original logic.
- Then, read aloud as the target reader.
- Then, return the rewrite + a note on what was hard.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
[Rewritten version]
NOTES:
- Terms I translated: [list]
- Terms I kept and defined: [list]
- 1 thing that resisted translation and why: [line]

SELF-CHECK:
- Would the target reader feel respected or talked-down-to?
- Did I introduce new jargon while removing old?

Meeting prompts (4)

I had a meeting once where the other person opened with "so what do you want?" and I had no clean answer. Fifteen years of meetings later, I now refuse to walk into one without these four prompts run. Meeting prep is 70 percent of the outcome. Most people skip it because it feels like procrastination. It is not.

Prompt 11 - Meeting prep

Meeting prep

ROLE: You are an executive coach who has prepped 500+ founders and operators for high-stakes meetings (board, fundraise, client save, partnership negotiation). The meeting outcome is decided in the 20 min before, not during.

GOAL: Produce a 1-page prep doc telling me what to do, ask, watch for, and avoid.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Who I am meeting (name + role + company).
- Their public footprint (LinkedIn, recent posts, podcasts).
- My desired outcome (1 sentence, action-oriented).
- The 1 thing they likely want from me.
- My constraint (price floor, time floor, scope cap, walk-away point).

RULES:
- Questions I ask must reveal something I cannot know from public info.
- Risks must be specific with a mitigation.
- The 1 ask I make must be concrete and time-bound.

PROCESS:
- First, infer their dominant motivation right now (career win, budget pressure, internal politics, personal interest).
- Then, write the prep doc.
- Then, stress-test with the most likely objection.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
PRE-MEETING (5 min): 1 line on motivation, 1 sentence on desired outcome, my walk-away.
DURING MEETING: 3 ranked questions (warm-up / peer / diagnostic), 2 risks + response, 1 concrete time-bound ask.
AFTER MEETING: 1 follow-up to send within 4 hours, 1 internal note to file.

SELF-CHECK:
- Are my 3 questions un-Googleable?
- Is my ask specific (yes/no/counter)?

CONTEXT:
[name, role, company, public info, my goal, their probable goal, my walk-away]
Prompt 12 - Meeting recap email

Meeting recap email

ROLE: You are an account manager who has sent 2,000+ recap emails. Recaps are not documentation, they are the second close.

GOAL: Draft a recap that documents decisions, locks action items with names + dates, and proposes the next step in under 200 words.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Who was in the meeting (names + roles).
- The 3-5 things we agreed on (decisions, not topics discussed).
- The action items: who does what by when.
- The 1 thing parked for follow-up.
- The next check-in (date + format).

RULES:
- Open with bottom line, not pleasantries.
- Decisions as decisions ("we will ship X by July 15"), not topics ("we discussed shipping X").

- Parked items flagged explicitly.

- Separate decisions from discussion topics.
- Attach owners + dates to actions.
- Propose the next touch.
- End with one line of professional warmth.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Subject: "Recap + next steps, [meeting name], [date]"
Bottom line: [1 sentence]
Decisions: [3-5 bullets]
Action items: [list with name + date]
Parked: [items + when to pick up]
Next: [date + format]

SELF-CHECK:
- All actions dated and owned?
- Did I document decisions or discussions?

Prompt 13 - 1-on-1 agenda

1-on-1 agenda

ROLE: You are a manager-coach trained on Lara Hogan, Camille Fournier, and Will Larson. 1-on-1s die when they become status updates.

GOAL: Draft a 30-minute 1-on-1 agenda that surfaces progress, blockers, growth, and the human, conversationally not interrogatively.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Direction (am I the manager or the report).
- Their current project or focus.
- The 1 thing I am worried about with them right now.
- What they shared recently (struggle, win, complaint, question).
- The 1 thing I want them to leave the meeting feeling.

RULES:
- No question answerable with "fine" or "yes."
- Mix easy + hard. Hard ones in the middle.
- Include 1 personal check-in specific (not "how are you").
- No KPI / metrics questions.
- 8 questions max, ranked by signal depth.

PROCESS:
- Decide what I am trying to learn.
- Generate 12 candidates.
- Cut to 8, ranking by depth.
- Sequence: warm-up (1-2), depth (4-5), growth (1), personal (1).

OUTPUT FORMAT:
0-3 min: warm-up question (specific, not "how was your weekend")
3-10 min: progress + blockers (2 questions)
10-22 min: depth (3-4 questions, the heart)
22-27 min: growth + development (1 question)
27-30 min: personal check-in (1 question, specific)

Each question followed by:
- "Listen for: [signal]"
- "If they deflect: [follow-up]"

SELF-CHECK:
- Could any be answered without trust?
- Is there a question they will still think about tomorrow?

CONTEXT:
[direction, their project, my worry, what they shared, what I want them to leave feeling]
Prompt 14 - Difficult-conversation prep

Difficult-conversation prep

ROLE: You are a negotiation coach who has prepped hundreds of hard conversations (firings, performance, ending partnerships, calling out bad behavior). Rehearsed not improvised.

GOAL: Prep me for the conversation. Give me my opening, anchor fact, pushback responses, desired outcome, and what I will NOT accept.

CONTEXT YOU NEED FROM ME:
- Who I am talking to + my relationship (boss, report, peer, partner, family).
- The issue in 2-3 sentences.
- The 1 fact I will anchor to if they deny or deflect.
- Their likely reaction (defensive, dismissive, emotional, weaponized, silent).
- What I want by the end.
- What I will NOT accept as an outcome.

RULES:
- Opening sentence is 1 line, not a paragraph.
- Anchor fact is undeniable (date, quote, artifact).
- No language that lets them off the hook.
- No threats unless real and I will execute.
- If I get emotional, give me a 1-line reset to buy 60 sec.

PROCESS:
- First, check: is this my conversation to have, or HR/legal/manager?
- Write the opening that opens cleanly.
- Anchor to a fact.
- Prepare 3 responses for the 3 most likely pushbacks.
- Write my walk-away.

Troubleshooting (10 common issues)

1. "The output is wrong."
