## 1. The Platform Reality

LinkedIn is a professional network that rewards depth over brevity — but only when that depth earns its keep. The algorithm favors posts that generate early comments (within the first 60–90 minutes). Boubacar's audience here is senior executives, founders, HR leaders, and consultants who are skeptical of generic content and hungry for the specific observation they haven't heard yet.

The goal on LinkedIn is not to go viral. The goal is to be the person executives think of when they need a consultant for the thing Boubacar does. Every post should advance that positioning — directly or indirectly.

## 2. Post Length Tiers

### Tier 1: Short (under 300 characters)
**Best for:** A sharp observation that needs no setup. A provocative claim. A question worth sitting with.

**Format:** One to two sentences, no line breaks, no formatting. Let it breathe as a block of text.

**When to use:** When the idea is strong enough to stand alone. When you're responding to something happening in the news or in business culture. When you want a high-comment-to-impression ratio.

**Example:**
> Accountability without authority is just blame with better vocabulary.

---

### Tier 2: Medium (300–1,000 characters)
**Best for:** A single insight with one supporting story or observation. A before/after framing. A contrast between two approaches.

**Format:** 3–6 short paragraphs. Line breaks between each paragraph. No bullet lists unless absolutely necessary (prefer prose).

**When to use:** 80% of Boubacar's LinkedIn posts should be here. It's the primary format — long enough to demonstrate depth, short enough to read in under 90 seconds.

---

### Tier 3: Long-form Article (1,000+ words)
**Best for:** A framework. A structured argument with evidence. A piece designed to be saved, shared in Slack, and referenced by name ("the article Boubacar wrote about decision latency").

**When to use:** Maximum once per month. Only when the idea genuinely requires the space. Not as a substitute for multiple shorter posts.

**Format:** Use LinkedIn's native article editor. Structure with clear H2 subheads. No corporate language. Write it the way Boubacar would explain it across a table.

## 3. Hook Line Formula (The "See More" Cutoff)

LinkedIn cuts post text at approximately 210–220 characters before the "...see more" link. The hook must be entirely contained in those characters — it cannot rely on what comes after the cut.

### The hook job
1. Create a question in the reader's head that only the rest of the post can answer
2. State something that sounds wrong enough to warrant verification
3. Signal that this post is for *them* specifically (the role, the problem, the moment)

### Hook formulas that work

**The Diagnosis:**
> "Most change programs don't fail at execution. They fail the moment a senior leader is asked to change their own behavior."

**The Contrast:**
> "Companies invest in strategy frameworks. Then they hold the quarterly budget meeting that invalidates the whole thing."

**The Specific Anomaly:**
> "I've worked with 40+ organizations across 12 countries. The bottleneck is almost never what the assessment says it is."

**The Direct Address:**
> "If your org chart changed in the last 18 months and your decision rights didn't — you didn't restructure. You rearranged."

**What to avoid in hooks:**
- Questions that can be answered with "it depends" (because then the reader doesn't need to click)
- Buzzword openings: "In today's fast-changing world..." — immediately disqualifying
- Humble openers: "I wanted to share a quick thought..." — starts in apology mode
- Numbers without stakes: "3 things I learned from..." — works only if the specific number matters

## 4. Formatting: Line Breaks, Spacing, Bullets vs. Prose

### Line breaks
Every paragraph gets its own line break. No walls of text. LinkedIn's interface makes long paragraphs visually dense — shorter paragraphs read as more confident and more senior.

Single-sentence paragraphs are fine. Encouraged, actually.

### Spacing
One blank line between every paragraph. Never two blank lines (looks like padding). Never no breaks (looks like a wall).

### Bullets vs. prose
**Default to prose.** Boubacar's voice is a voice — it has rhythm and flow. Bullet lists interrupt that.

**Use bullets only when:**
- The items are genuinely list-like (e.g., 5 specific signs, not 5 connected ideas)
- The list has 4+ items that would become run-on sentences in prose
- Each bullet is a standalone insight, not part of a flowing argument

**Never use:**
- Nested bullets (sub-bullets signal a PowerPoint, not a person)
- Bullet lists as the entire post body (feels like a framework dump, not a thought)
- Emoji bullets (🔥, ✅, 👉) — not in Boubacar's register

### Bold text
Use sparingly. The key phrase per paragraph only, not for decoration. If everything is bold, nothing is.

### Emojis
Use zero to two per post, maximum. If used at all, place at the end of a sentence as punctuation — never at the start of a line as a bullet substitute.

## 5. Voice Calibration for LinkedIn

LinkedIn is slightly more formal than X — but only slightly. The difference is register, not vocabulary. Boubacar on LinkedIn sounds like Boubacar in a first meeting with a C-suite client: direct, prepared, measured. Not stiff.

### Adjustments from X voice
- Posts can have a beginning, middle, and end (X posts are all middle)
- One personal story per week is appropriate; more than that risks the feed feeling like a memoir
- Data and evidence can be cited — not mandatory, but appropriate when available
- The landing line (final sentence) carries extra weight on LinkedIn — make it the most quotable sentence in the post

### What stays the same from X voice
- No buzzword soup
- No excited preambles
- Specific examples beat general principles
- Contrast structures ("not X... Y") remain highly effective
- The conclusion comes first or second, not last

## 6. Post Type Selection

| Post type | Use when |
|---|---|
| **Personal story** | The story has a point that transfers to the reader's situation. Not just a thing that happened. The lesson must be explicit and non-obvious. |
| **Insight / observation** | You've seen a pattern across multiple clients or situations. State the pattern, then give the evidence. |
| **Opinion** | You disagree with conventional wisdom on something specific. Name the conventional view explicitly. Disagree with precision. |
| **Data / case study** | You have a number or a named outcome that makes the point concrete. Lead with the data, then the interpretation. |
| **Framework** | Only in long-form articles. Never in a medium post. A framework in 800 characters is a list. |

## 7. Call-to-Action Patterns

### The CTA problem on LinkedIn
Most LinkedIn CTAs are either invisible (no ask at all) or needy ("DM me to work together!"). Neither works.

### Boubacar's CTA register
The CTA should sound like an offer, not a pitch. It emerges from the post, it doesn't get bolted on.

**Effective CTA patterns:**

**The implicit offer:**
> "If your organization is working through a restructuring and the decision rights aren't keeping up — that's usually where we start."

**The real question:**
> "What's the decision that's been on your list for six months that you haven't made yet?" (This generates replies, which feeds the algorithm and builds relationships)

**The direct but non-pressured invite:**
> "I work with executive teams on exactly this. If it's relevant — my calendar is linked in the comments."

**What to avoid:**
- "Follow me for more insights like this" — transactional and weak
- "Like and share if you agree" — engagement bait
- "DM me 'READY' to get my free guide" — marketing bot energy
- Tagging 5 people "who might find this useful" — spam

## 8. Five Example Posts in Boubacar's Voice

**Topic: Organizational restructuring (medium format)**

> A client spent nine months redesigning their org chart.
>
> New reporting lines. New titles. New leadership team photos on the intranet.
>
> The first major decision after the relaunch took three weeks and two escalations to resolve.
>
> The structure changed. The decision architecture didn't.
>
> Restructuring isn't reorganizing boxes. It's redesigning who decides what, at what speed, with what information. Everything else is cosmetic.
>
> Most organizations do the cosmetic part and wonder why behavior doesn't change.

---

**Topic: Consulting and client relationships (short format)**

> The most useful thing a consultant can do is say the thing the room already knows but won't say to each other.
>
> That's not insight. That's permission.

---

**Topic: African leadership context (medium format)**

> There's a version of "leadership development" that gets exported to African organizations from Western business schools.
>
> It teaches individual achievement. Self-advocacy. Separating personal relationships from professional decisions.
>
> Some of it is useful. Some of it breaks teams that were working.
>
> The organizations I respect most in West Africa don't imitate the Silicon Valley model. They run on high-context, relational decision-making — and they've figured out when to use it and when to shift gears.
>
> That's not underdevelopment. That's a different kind of sophistication.

---

**Topic: Strategy and budget misalignment (short format)**

> Strategy lives in Q1 planning.
>
> Budget lives in Q4 conversations.
>
> They have never officially met.

---

**Topic: Performance and accountability (medium format)**

> I was brought in to diagnose a "performance problem" on a senior team.
>
> The data showed consistent missed targets. Everyone agreed the team wasn't performing.
>
> Six weeks in, I found the real issue: three of the five people on that team had conflicting mandates they'd received from the same executive.
>
> They weren't underperforming. They were stuck in a system designed to produce conflict.
>
> Performance problems are often structural problems wearing a personal label. The diagnosis changes everything about the fix.

## 9. Anti-Patterns: What Kills Engagement on LinkedIn

| Anti-pattern | What it signals | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm humbled and honored to announce..." | Passive and self-important simultaneously | State the news directly, one sentence |
| Long inspirational quote from someone else as the entire post | No original thinking | Use the quote as evidence, not the post |
| "Let's connect!" as a CTA on every post | Transactional | Earn the connection; it happens naturally |
| Posting a job description as a "value-add" | Filler content | Comment on what the hiring pattern reveals |
| Reposting company press releases verbatim | Promotional, not valuable | React to the news; give the business interpretation |
| Ending every post with a 3-question survey | Engagement hacking | Ask one real question or none |
| Photo of yourself at a conference with a vague caption | Low-effort visibility play | The insight from the day, not the proof you attended |
| "What do you think?" as the only CTA | No stakes | Ask a specific, answerable question |
| Posting at 6am local every day regardless of quality | Consistency theater | 3 excellent posts > 7 average posts |
| Vague thought leadership ("The key to success is...") | Sounds like everyone else | Specific claim with specific evidence |

## 9. POST FORMATTING SPEC

### Line Break Cadence — The LinkedIn White Space Technique

LinkedIn collapses posts after ~210 characters with a "see more" cutoff. The visual rhythm of line breaks determines whether people expand the post.

**Rule:** One sentence per line. One blank line between every 2–3 lines. Never more than 3 lines in a row without a break.

```
Most companies don't have a growth problem.

They have a clarity problem.

They can't describe what they do in a way that makes the right person say:
"That's exactly what I need."

Here's how to fix that in one afternoon.
```

**Never:**
```
Most companies don't have a growth problem. They have a clarity problem. They can't describe what they do in a way that makes the right person say "That's exactly what I need." Here's how to fix that in one afternoon.
```

### Hook Line Rules

The hook is the line before "see more." It must work as a standalone sentence.

- **Max length:** 120 characters
- **No punctuation at the end** of the hook line (period is fine, question mark is fine, nothing is fine — never ellipsis)
- **Never start with "I"** — LinkedIn's algorithm and readers both respond better to "You", a number, or a direct claim
- **Hook formats that work:**

| Format | Example |
|---|---|
| Contrarian claim | Most consultants are solving the wrong problem |
| Specific number | 3 things I stopped doing that doubled my close rate |
| Direct observation | The best clients I've ever had all said the same thing |
| Named problem | Your pitch deck isn't the problem |
| Question (use sparingly) | What does it actually cost to have the wrong strategy? |

### Paragraph Max Length

- **3 lines maximum** per paragraph block before a visual break
- Single-sentence paragraphs are encouraged — they read as punchy and intentional
- Never write a paragraph that would require scrolling to finish on mobile

### Bold Text on LinkedIn

LinkedIn doesn't support markdown bold. To use **bold**, paste Unicode bold characters.

**Tool:** Use a Unicode bold converter (e.g. YayText or similar)
**When to use bold:** One phrase per post maximum. The key term or insight only.
**Never bold:** Entire sentences, your name, generic words like "important" or "key"

```
𝗠𝗼𝘀𝘁 𝗳𝗼𝘂𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗿𝘀 don't have a sales problem.   ✓ (one bolded phrase)
𝗠𝗼𝘀𝘁 𝗳𝗼𝘂𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗿𝘀 𝗱𝗼𝗻'𝘁 𝗵𝗮𝘃𝗲 𝗮 𝘀𝗮𝗹𝗲𝘀 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗯𝗹𝗲𝗺.  ✗ (whole sentence bolded)
```

### Emoji Rules for LinkedIn

More conservative than X. Emoji on LinkedIn signals informality — use only when it adds meaning.

- **Max:** 1 emoji per post, end of post only
- **Acceptable contexts:** Announcing something genuinely good, a thread-style list where bullets are replaced by →
- **Never:** Decorative emoji, emoji in the hook line, multiple emoji in sequence

**Approved:**
- → (for list items or implication)
- ✓ (for checklist-style posts)

**Avoid entirely:** 🔥 💯 🚀 🙏 👏 💪 (signal low-effort content on LinkedIn)

### Article vs Post Formatting Differences

| Element | Post | Article |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 150–1,300 chars | 800–2,500 words |
| Hook | Before "see more" cutoff | H1 title + subhead |
| Sections | Line breaks only | H2 headers required |
| Images | Optional cover image | Cover image required |
| Links | End of post | Inline + end of article |
| CTA | 1 line, optional | Final section, specific |
| Tone | Conversational, direct | Still direct, slightly more structured |

### CTA Line Formatting

One line. End of post. Optional — not every post needs one.

**Formats that work:**
```
If this is where you are, let's talk.
The diagnostic takes 2 hours. catalystworks.com/diagnostic
What's the constraint you're working around right now?
```

**Formats to avoid:**
```
Like, comment, and share if you found this valuable! 🙏   ✗
Drop a "YES" in the comments if this resonates!           ✗
Follow me for more content like this.                     ✗
Click the link in my bio.                                 ✗
```
