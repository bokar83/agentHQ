## Section 1: The Landscape

### 1.1 The Governance Triangle

Three overlapping disciplines that clients conflate constantly. Clarity here signals expertise fast.

- **IT Governance**: The factory. Infrastructure, availability, cybersecurity, technology-to-strategy alignment. Keeps the systems running and safe from outside attack.
- **Data Governance**: The raw materials. Data quality, lineage, access controls, lifecycle. Ensures data is accurate and usable.
- **AI Governance**: The finished product. The unique behaviors and risks of algorithms. Ethics, bias, explainability, accountability, human oversight. Governs how decisions get made by non-human agents.

The core truth: you cannot have credible AI governance without reliable data governance. You cannot have reliable data governance without functional IT governance. They stack.

### 1.2 The Six Core Principles in Practice

1. **Accountability**: Every deployed AI system has a named human owner.
2. **Transparency**: Users know when they are interacting with AI or AI-generated output.
3. **Fairness**: Models are tested for discriminatory bias before launch.
4. **Safety and Security**: Protection against adversarial attacks and against shadow AI (employees uploading sensitive data to unapproved tools).
5. **Explainability**: Constrained, rule-based AI workflows are preferred over black-box decision models for high-stakes actions.
6. **Human Oversight**: Workflows are designed so AI drafts, humans approve.

### 1.3 The Legal and Framework Map (verified as of April 2026)

**The Utah AI Policy Act (SB 149).** Effective May 1, 2024. The first US state law regulating private-sector use of generative AI. Applies to any individual or entity using generative AI to interact with people in Utah, regardless of where the organization is located. Administrative fines up to $2,500 per violation. For Catalyst Works specifically, this is the home-state authority play. Boubacar lives in Utah. The Utah AI Act has been in effect for nearly two years. Most Utah SMBs have not yet formally addressed it.

**The Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA / HB 149).** Effective January 1, 2026. Intent-based liability framework for AI misuse (unlawful discrimination, manipulation, deepfakes, child exploitation). Exclusive enforcement by the Texas Attorney General. Civil penalties up to $200,000 per violation with a 60-day cure period. Applies to any entity conducting business in Texas or offering products to Texas residents.

**Illinois HB 3773.** Effective January 1, 2026. Amends the Illinois Human Rights Act. Prohibits employers from using AI in ways that discriminate against protected classes. Requires employer disclosure when AI is used in recruitment, hiring, promotion, discipline, or termination decisions. Applies to employers with one or more employees in Illinois.

**The Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205).** Effective date: June 30, 2026 (after the delay from February 1, 2026 via SB 25B-004). Requires developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to use reasonable care to protect against algorithmic discrimination. Subject to active political revision; a repeal-and-replace bill was under consideration in early 2026. Colorado stakeholders should monitor state legislative action through mid-2026.

**The EU AI Act.** In force since August 1, 2024. Banned practices enforceable since February 2, 2025. GPAI obligations since August 2, 2025. High-risk system requirements scheduled for August 2, 2026, though AI Omnibus negotiations may push this to December 2027 or August 2028. Fines up to €35 million or 7% of worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher) for prohibited practices; up to €15 million or 3% for high-risk violations; up to €7.5 million or 1% for incorrect information.

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework.** US federal voluntary guidance. Four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. Not mandatory but increasingly referenced in litigation, procurement due diligence, and regulatory interpretation. Voluntary is a legal status, not a recommendation to skip it.

**ISO/IEC 42001.** International certifiable AI management system standard. Published 2023. Emerging as the "certification" version of AI governance for organizations that need a third-party verifiable claim. Catalyst Works does not claim 42001 expertise in year one; positions 42001 as a potential roadmap destination for Tier 3 clients.

**Federal preemption risk.** A December 11, 2025 executive order directed the DOJ to challenge state AI laws. Federal preemption typically requires congressional action. The legal landscape remains unstable. Messaging should anchor to operating-environment pressures (vendor questionnaires, client due diligence, insurance carrier requirements) rather than to specific regulatory deadlines that may move.

### 1.4 How SMBs Actually Implement Governance (and fail to)

The dominant implementation pattern in mid-market and enterprise is "top-down organizationally, bottom-up architecturally." Leadership sets the Acceptable Use Policy. Engineering implements an AI Gateway (a secure proxy that enforces rules technically).

For SMB owner-operators with 5 to 200 employees, the AI Gateway implementation is out of reach. No dedicated IT. No engineering team. The "top-down organizationally" part is the only lever available, and it has to be made to work without technical enforcement. This is the core design constraint for any SMB governance program and the reason enterprise-coded approaches fail in the SMB market.

**Common failure modes:**

1. **Wait-and-see paralysis.** Banning AI outright leads employees to use unsanctioned personal tools. Shadow AI is infinitely riskier than governed AI because it leaves no documentation trail, no training opportunity, no incident response path.
2. **Vendor blindspot.** Owners vet the primary LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude) while ignoring that their CRM, HR platform, accounting software, and project management tools have silently added AI features trained on customer data.
3. **Template theater.** Generic AI policies downloaded from the internet and stored on a shared drive. Signed once, never read again, not reflective of actual operations. Worse than no policy because it creates a paper record of compliance that fails under scrutiny.
4. **Decision-owner ambiguity.** No one is named accountable. When something goes wrong, the response is reactive and political rather than procedural.

### 1.5 The Anatomy of a Working SMB AI Policy

Seven sections, each one concrete enough that an employee can use it without asking a question.

1. **Purpose and Scope**: Why the policy exists and who it applies to.
2. **Risk Classification System**: Low / Medium / High / Prohibited, with one-sentence rules for each tier.
3. **Acceptable Use Rules**: What employees can use AI for, what they cannot.
4. **Data Handling and Privacy**: What data is explicitly prohibited from entering AI tools (client PII, financial data, protected health information, contract terms, etc.).
5. **Vendor Procurement Addendums**: The three clauses every AI vendor contract must include or disclose.
6. **Human Oversight Matrix**: What decisions require human review before AI output is delivered.
7. **Incident Response Protocol**: What happens when AI produces an error that affects a client.

Review triggers, not review calendars. The policy gets refreshed when a new regulation takes effect, a new AI vendor category is adopted, or an incident occurs. Not on a quarterly schedule that nobody remembers.

## Section 2: The SHIELD Methodology

SHIELD is the Catalyst Works-owned methodology for AI Risk and Readiness Assessment. Six steps. Delivered in 14 days. $3,500 fixed.

- **S** (Surface): Complete AI tool inventory. Every tool touching the business, including embedded AI in software already in use.
- **H** (Halt): Identify the highest-risk tools and the highest-risk uses. Pause them where necessary. Document what's happening in the meantime.
- **I** (Inventory): Formal register with risk tier, accountable owner, data types, vendor terms summary.
- **E** (Educate): Employee communication that translates the policy into plain-language rules the team can actually follow.
- **L** (Lock): Written Acceptable Use Policy and vendor approval process, customized to the client's industry and jurisdiction.
- **D** (Document): Final deliverable with Governance Maturity Briefing and Margin and Operational Risk Indicators section.

The SHIELD deliverable is the foot-in-the-door product. The Margin and Operational Risk Indicators section is the bridge.

## Toolkit Section 1: The Discovery Session

### 1.1 Session Architecture

This is a 60-minute structured working session, not a sales call. The frame matters: you are conducting a diagnostic, not pitching a service.

Call it what it is: an AI Governance Diagnostic Session.

| Phase              | Time      | What Happens                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ------------------ | --------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Open               | 0-5 min   | Frame the session. Set the agenda. Establish that you are here to understand their situation, not to sell them anything today. One sentence on what you do, then stop talking about yourself.                                                |
| Current State      | 5-20 min  | Diagnostic questions: AI usage inventory, risk exposure, existing policies. Listen more than you speak. Take visible notes. Surface the gap between what they think they have and what they actually have.                                   |
| Accountability Map | 20-30 min | Identify who owns what. Who is responsible for AI decisions today? Who gets called if something goes wrong? What happens when an employee uses an unapproved tool? This is where most SMBs discover they have no answers.                    |
| Vendor Exposure    | 30-40 min | Walk through their AI vendor stack. For each tool: what data flows into it? What does the vendor's contract say about retention and training? Most clients have not read these clauses.                                                      |
| Maturity Score     | 40-50 min | Score them live on the maturity model (Section 1.3). Do this visibly. Live scoring creates a shared reference point and signals measurement, not improvisation.                                                                              |
| Closing Move       | 50-60 min | Reflect back what you heard. Name the top two or three gaps. Present the output template (Section 1.4) and explain it will arrive within 48 hours. Close with one specific question: "Is fixing this in your top five priorities right now?" |

### 1.2 Opening Framing Script

"Today I want to understand how your organization is actually using AI, who is accountable for those decisions, and where your exposure is. I am going to ask specific questions and take notes. At the end, I will give you a score on a governance maturity scale and tell you the two or three things that need attention most urgently. You will have a written summary within 48 hours. Sound useful?"

### 1.3 Closing Move (The One Question)

"Based on what we just found, [name the top gap specifically: 'you have no approved vendor list and three tools with client data in them'] is closing that kind of exposure in your top priorities right now? Because if it is, I have a specific engagement that addresses exactly this."

### 1.4 Diagnostic Questions by Category

These are the exact questions. Do not ask all of them. Use the first one in each category to gauge depth, then follow the thread. Your job is to surface the gap, not exhaust the topic.

**CATEGORY A: Current AI Usage**

- A1: Walk me through every AI tool your team uses on a regular basis, including tools built into software you already use (AI features in your CRM, your email, your accounting software).
- A2: Are there tools your employees are using informally, outside of anything you have officially approved?
- A3: Which of these tools touches client data, employee data, or proprietary business information?
- A4: Who made the decision to adopt each of these tools, and what was the approval process?
- A5: Is there an AI system that is making or significantly influencing decisions that used to be made by a human?

**CATEGORY B: Risk Exposure**

- B1: If one of your AI tools gave a client wrong information or made a discriminatory decision, what would happen? Who would handle it, and what is your liability exposure?
- B2: Have you had any situation where an AI tool produced an output that surprised you, embarrassed you, or caused a problem?
- B3: If a client asked you today how you use AI and what data protections you have in place, what would you say?
- B4: Are any of your clients in regulated industries (healthcare, financial services, legal, HR)? Do you know if your AI use creates compliance obligations for them?
- B5: What would a data breach involving an AI vendor cost you financially, reputationally, in client relationships?

**CATEGORY C: Existing Policies**

- C1: Do you have a written policy that governs how your team uses AI? If yes, where does it live and when was it last updated?
- C2: Does your employee handbook say anything about AI use?
- C3: What data are employees explicitly prohibited from inputting into AI tools? Is this written anywhere or understood informally?
- C4: If I asked one of your employees tomorrow what they are and are not allowed to do with AI, what would they say?
- C5: Have you reviewed your client contracts recently to check whether your AI use is consistent with your data handling obligations?

**CATEGORY D: Stakeholder Accountability**

- D1: If something goes wrong with an AI system tomorrow, who is the first call? Who owns the response?
- D2: Who in your organization has authority to approve or reject the adoption of a new AI tool?
- D3: Does your leadership team have a shared understanding of what your AI risk exposure is?
- D4: Has AI governance ever come up in a board meeting, an investor conversation, or a client due diligence process?
- D5: If a regulator or auditor asked you to demonstrate responsible AI use, could you produce evidence of that within 48 hours?

**CATEGORY E: Vendor Relationships**

- E1: Have you read the AI-specific data provisions in your vendor contracts? Specifically: does your vendor use your data to train their models?
- E2: Do you have a list of AI vendors your team is approved to use? Who maintains it?
- E3: When you evaluate a new AI software vendor, what specifically do you ask about their AI practices?
- E4: If a vendor's AI system failed or was compromised, what notification would you receive and how quickly?
- E5: Do you have any contractual protections (indemnification, limitation of liability) covering AI-related vendor failures?

## Toolkit Section 2: Objection Handling

Do not rehearse these as scripts. Internalize the logic. The goal is genuine persuasion, not clever comebacks. Each response reframes the objection without dismissing it.

### 2.1 "We're too small to need this."

The NYC MyCity chatbot that gave small business owners advice to withhold workers' tips and ignore other labor laws was not an enterprise problem. Size determines the complexity of governance, not whether you need it. A three-person firm with one AI tool touching client data is exposed. The scale of the governance matches the scale of the operation, which is exactly what I help design.

### 2.2 "We don't really use AI that much."

Does your CRM have AI features? Does your email platform? Your accounting software? AI is embedded in most business software now, and you may be using it without knowing it. The question is not whether you use AI. It's whether you know how it's using your data.

### 2.3 "We already have a privacy policy."

A privacy policy covers how you handle data you collect. It does not cover how your employees use AI tools, what data they put into those tools, how your vendors use that data, or who is accountable when an AI system makes a wrong call. Those are four separate governance gaps that a privacy policy does not touch.

### 2.4 "This feels like compliance overhead."

Here's the alternative: one of your employees puts a client contract into an AI tool whose vendor uses it to train their model. Your client finds out. You have no policy, no documentation, no accountability structure. The conversation you have then costs more (legal exposure, lost client trust, management time) than governance would have. This is not overhead. It's insurance that you pay once and use forever.

### 2.5 "We'll deal with this when regulations require it."

Texas TRAIGA went live January 1, 2026. Illinois HB 3773 went live the same day. Colorado's AI Act takes effect June 30, 2026. Utah's AI Policy Act has been live since May 1, 2024. If you have employees or clients in those states, it's already required. And the organizations that wait for a regulatory trigger always pay more to remediate than they would have spent to build it right the first time. Reactive governance is always more expensive than proactive governance.

### 2.6 "We don't have budget for this right now."

What's your legal bill if one of your vendors has an AI data incident and you're named in the client complaint? What does it cost you if a prospective client asks about your AI practices in a due diligence call and you have no answer? Governance is not a discretionary expense. It's a hedge against a specific class of operational and legal risk. What I do is find the minimum viable version that gives you that protection at a price that makes sense for your operation.

### 2.7 "Can't I just use a template I find online?"

You can. The same way you can use a contract template you find online. The risk is that a generic template does not reflect your specific AI tool stack, your actual data practices, your real vendor relationships, or the laws that apply to your specific jurisdiction and industry. And a governance policy that does not reflect your actual operations is not just useless. It's actively misleading if it ever gets scrutinized.

## Toolkit Section 3: The Business Case for Skeptical Owner-Operators

Anchor every conversation to outcomes the client already cares about. Do not lead with compliance. Lead with what keeps them awake.

### 3.1 Liability

If an AI tool you use makes a wrong decision (misclassifies a client, gives bad advice, discriminates in hiring) and you have no governance documentation, you cannot demonstrate that you acted reasonably. Governance is what creates a paper trail of responsible practice. Without it, you are exposed. With it, you have a defense.

### 3.2 Operational Continuity

What happens when the AI tool your team depends on changes its pricing, gets acquired, or changes its data practices? A governance program includes vendor risk management. It tells you what tools you're dependent on, what your exit strategy is, and what the data implications of a vendor change are. Organizations without this find out the hard way.

### 3.3 Vendor Risk

Most SMBs have signed AI vendor contracts without reading the AI-specific clauses. Some vendors explicitly reserve the right to use your data to train their models. Some do not notify you of incidents unless required by law (which varies by jurisdiction). A governance program includes a vendor assessment process that asks the right questions before you're locked in.

### 3.4 Employee Behavior

Your employees are making decisions today about what data they put into AI tools. Without a written policy, those decisions are ungoverned. An employee sending client financials to an AI tool to generate a report is not malicious. They're solving a problem with the tools available to them. Governance gives them a framework so they solve problems in ways that don't create new ones.

### 3.5 Competitive Positioning

Your enterprise clients and prospects are starting to ask about AI governance in due diligence. If you cannot answer "What is your AI governance policy?" you are at a disadvantage against competitors who can. Governance is increasingly a procurement requirement, not just an ethical nice-to-have.

## Toolkit Section 5: The Three-Tier Implementation Roadmap

### 5.1 Tier 1: Minimum Viable Governance ("Not Exposed")

**Target:** 1-50 employees. AI adoption is opportunistic. No dedicated IT or compliance function. Primary concern: not being caught flat-footed.

**Deliverables:**

- Complete AI inventory (every tool, every use, every data type)
- Acceptable Use Policy (what's permitted, what's prohibited, who approves new tools)
- Named accountable owner for each tool

**Time to implement:** 30-60 days with active client participation. 2-3 working sessions plus async review.

**Common blockers:** Client cannot find time to complete inventory. Leadership has not agreed on "prohibited use." Vendor contracts not accessible or unsigned PDFs in someone's inbox.

**Signs ready for Tier 2:** Inventory complete and current. Policy written, distributed, acknowledged. At least one accountable owner per tool. Client has adopted at least one new AI tool and used the approval process to do it.

### 5.2 Tier 2: Operational Governance ("Systematically Managed")

**Target:** 50-250 employees. AI embedded in multiple business functions. Some regulatory exposure or client due diligence requirements. Needs governance that works across teams.

**Deliverables:**

- Full multi-section governance policy
- Risk classification for all AI systems in inventory
- Human oversight protocols by risk tier
- Vendor management process with standard assessment checklist
- Incident response plan
- Role-specific employee training
- Quarterly review cadence established

**Time to implement:** 60-120 days. Cross-functional participation (legal, operations, HR, leadership). 4-6 working sessions plus document review.

**Common blockers:** Legal review delays. Disagreement on "high-risk" in this organization. Resistance from department heads who feel the policy constrains their teams. Training rollout competing with other organizational priorities.

**Signs ready for Tier 3:** Policy functional, not just documented. Staff can answer "what does our policy say about X" without looking it up. At least one incident has been processed through the response protocol. Client has used governance to evaluate and reject or approve a new vendor.

### 5.3 Tier 3: Mature Governance ("Differentiated")

**Target:** 250-500 employees, or high-stakes regulated industries at any size. AI is a strategic function. Governance is a competitive differentiator. May serve enterprise clients who require governance evidence.

**Deliverables:**

- ISO 42001 alignment (not necessarily certification)
- AI ethics statement for external communication
- Governance report for board or leadership
- Bias audit process for high-risk systems
- Governance-as-trust-signal communications strategy
- Vendor contract clause library
- Annual governance review as a formal event

**Time to implement:** Ongoing. Initial build 90-180 days. Governance is now a management function, not a project.

**Common blockers:** No one owns the ongoing governance function. Review cadence breaks down when leadership attention is elsewhere. Documentation drifts out of sync with actual adoption. ISO alignment requires organizational change beyond documentation.

**Signs Tier 3 is working:** Governance is referenced in sales materials and client conversations. New AI tool adoption is self-governed by staff using the established process. Leadership can answer "what is our AI governance maturity?" with a specific, evidence-backed answer.

## Toolkit Section 6: Designing Governance That Stays Relevant

The failure mode is a policy written in 2026 that's still in version 1.0 in 2028 while the organization has adopted twelve new AI tools. These mechanisms prevent that.

- **Amendment triggers baked into the policy text** (not a separate process): specific events that automatically trigger review. New regulation. New AI vendor category. AI incident. Organizational restructure.
- **AI inventory review as a quarterly operations item:** tie it to an existing meeting (quarterly business review, operations review) so it doesn't depend on someone remembering.
- **Tool adoption as review trigger:** every time the organization adopts a new AI tool, the inventory and risk classification is the gate. No inventory update, no tool approval.
- **Sunset clauses on vendor assessments:** assessments expire after 12 months and must be renewed. Forces regular contact with vendor data practices.
- **Living document format:** policies stored in version-controlled documents (Notion, Google Docs with version history). PDFs are archives. Living documents are systems.
- **Regulatory monitoring:** build in a named person responsible for monitoring AI regulatory developments in the organization's jurisdictions. Can be a Google Alert or a paid monitoring service. Must be someone's job.

## Toolkit Section 7: Content Angles and Educational Arc

### 7.1 The Most Misunderstood Concepts in AI Governance

Each one is a content piece waiting to exist.

- **"AI governance" and "AI ethics" are not the same.** Ethics is the values. Governance is the system that operationalizes values inside an organization. Ethics without governance is aspiration. Governance without ethical grounding is compliance theater.
- **Having a privacy policy does not mean you have AI governance.** A privacy policy governs how you handle data you collect from users. It does not govern how your employees use AI tools, what data they input, or how vendors use that data.
- **"Low risk" AI is not "no risk" AI.** Risk classification must account for scale and breadth of use, not just individual system risk level.
- **NIST AI RMF is voluntary, not optional.** Voluntary means no penalty for non-adoption. It does not mean no consequences. Organizations facing litigation, regulatory scrutiny, or client due diligence without a recognized framework are at a disadvantage.
- **"The vendor handles it" is not a governance answer.** Vendors handle their own data practices. They do not govern how your employees use the tool, what data they input, or who's accountable inside your organization for the AI system's outputs.
- **Governance is not a one-time project.** AI capabilities change quarterly. Regulations evolve monthly. A governance document written once and never reviewed is outdated by the time the ink dries.

Gap: governance resources are written for organizations with a compliance team; no one has produced a week-by-week sequence for a two-person operation.

4. **State AI Law Tracker for SMBs.** Regularly updated reference page plus email alerts. Audience: SMB owners operating in multiple states. Gap: legal firms publish summaries for enterprise legal teams; no one has produced an SMB-readable tracker mapping laws to business impact.

## Toolkit Section 8: Mastery Tiering

### 8.1 Must Master Before First Paid Engagement

If you cannot speak fluently about all of this without notes, you're not ready for a paid engagement.

- **AI Governance fundamentals.** Define AI governance precisely and distinguish from data, IT, corporate governance. Explain all six core principles operationally. Resources: NIST AI RMF (nist.gov, 40 pages, free). EU AI Act executive summary.
- **Regulatory landscape.** Name Utah SB 149, Texas TRAIGA, Illinois HB 3773, Colorado SB 24-205, and the EU AI Act. Explain who each applies to and what it requires at a high level. Verify all regulatory details before client conversations.
- **Discovery session.** Run the full 60-minute session, score the maturity model live, produce the output template within 48 hours. Practice with two non-paying beta sessions before first paid engagement.
- **Objection handling.** Respond to all seven common objections without hesitation or script. Natural, not memorized. Say each one out loud five times.
- **Document anatomy.** Explain what every section of a complete AI policy covers and why it matters.
- **SMB positioning.** Articulate in 90 seconds what a diagnostic-led solo operator offers that larger firms cannot, using specific evidence.
- **SHIELD methodology.** Deliver the full methodology walkthrough in 5 minutes without reference.

### 8.2 Develop Through First 3-6 Months

These capabilities develop through real client exposure. Don't wait to accumulate before starting.

- **Industry-specific risk patterns.** After 5-10 clients, you will see patterns. Before that, research the industry before each engagement.
- **Vendor assessment depth.** Read three AI vendor contracts in full: OpenAI, Microsoft Copilot, Salesforce Einstein. Mark every clause on retention, training, incident notification.
- **Incident response design.** NIST SP 800-61 for the structural model. Adapt to AI context.
- **Policy language craft.** Write for specificity, not generality. Read three published AI policies from tech companies. Note what makes language specific vs generic.
- **Risk classification nuance.** Edge cases: tools that straddle tiers, tools low-risk individually but high-risk in aggregate, tools where risk depends on use rather than nature.
- **Content flywheel.** Produce consistent content while delivering. Build a capture system that logs insights from every client session for future content.

### 8.3 Long-Term Depth (12-24 Months)

These take time and become the moat.

- **ISO 42001 practitioner depth.** Purchase the ISO 42001 standard. Consider ISACA's implementation course if client demand warrants.
- **EU AI Act compliance advisory.** Study the Act article by article. Follow the European AI Office's guidance. Track Codes of Practice development.
- **Sector-specific expertise.** Develop deep competence in one or two sectors. Financial services: Federal Reserve SR 11-7. Healthcare: HHS AI guidelines. Professional services: ABA AI ethics opinions.
- **Algorithmic bias literacy.** "Fairness and Machine Learning" (Barocas, Hardt, Narayanan, fairmlbook.org, free). IBM AI Fairness 360 documentation.
- **Practitioner community standing.** IAPP AI governance community. ISACA AI Governance working group. Build referral flow, peer learning, early access to regulatory developments.

## Toolkit Section 9: Failure Modes in Practice

These are the places the practice can fail. Name them. Design against them.

### 9.1 Governance Becomes a Dead-End Engagement

**How it happens:** Assessment is delivered. Client treats it as a completed project. No next conversation. Relationship dies.

**Prevention:**

- Build the Margin and Operational Risk Indicators section into every deliverable. Not optional.
- Schedule the Strategic Briefing at contract signature, not engagement end. Next conversation on the calendar before Assessment is delivered.
- Strategic Briefing has a structured format ending with "Here are the two ways we'd work together next." No ambiguity.
- Pre-write Sprint and Retainer engagement letters. Send within 48 hours of Strategic Briefing.

### 9.2 Credential Gap Triggers Buyer Objection

**How it happens:** Prospect Googles Boubacar, finds no IAPP certification, no ISO 42001, no law degree. Concludes this is amateur work.

**Prevention:**

- Manufacture authority proof that doesn't require credentials: case studies, named methodology (SHIELD), published content, speaking appearances, self-published book by month 12.
- Get ahead of the objection in the Qualification Call: "I'm not a lawyer. What I do is not a substitute for legal advice. What I do is give you the operational infrastructure that makes your lawyer's job easier and your vendor conversations sharper."
- Build a "Who We Work With" page on catalystworks.consulting with testimonials from beta and early clients.

### 9.3 Regulatory Hallucination

**How it happens:** Practitioner cites a law or regulation that doesn't apply or cites it incorrectly. Client acts on bad information. Liability.

**Prevention:**

- Verify every regulatory citation before a client conversation. Primary sources only (state legislative websites, EU official text).
- Every deliverable includes a clear disclaimer: "This output is a starting point for governance documentation, not legal advice. Any policy affecting employee rights, client data, or regulatory compliance should be reviewed by qualified legal counsel before implementation."
- Maintain a "last verified" date on any regulatory summary in templates and lead magnets.

### 9.4 Build-Instead-of-Outreach Pattern

**How it happens:** Practitioner builds infrastructure (agent, microsite, YouTube production workflow) as a substitute for the anxiety-inducing work of sending messages to specific humans. This has already happened (30 cold emails, zero replies, March 2026).

**Prevention:**

- Weekly outreach quota is non-negotiable. 20 governance emails, 5 LinkedIn DMs, 2 podcast pitches. Every week. Tracked in The Forge 2.0.
- Content shipping does not count as outreach. Building does not count as outreach. Only messages sent to specific humans asking for specific conversations count.
- Monthly review includes an honest audit: what percentage of my working hours went to outreach vs building? If under 20%, correct immediately.

### 9.5 Scope Creep Into Technical AI Design

**How it happens:** Client asks practitioner to advise on AI system architecture, model selection, bias mitigation. Practitioner takes the work. Work is outside competency. Bad outcomes.

**Prevention:**

- Scope the practice explicitly to governance documentation and advisory. Technical AI design is out of scope.
- When a question falls outside scope ("how do I make my model less biased?"), redirect: "That's a technical AI question and outside what I do. What I can do is name the governance requirements around bias monitoring and help you write the vendor questions to ask your AI provider about their bias testing."

### 9.6 Price Anchoring Failure

**How it happens:** Practitioner takes the first engagement at a discount to get it signed. Discount becomes the reference price. All future clients anchor to the discount. Practice becomes unprofitable.

**Prevention:**

- $3,500 is the Assessment price. Not negotiable.
- If the first prospect wants a discount, offer a beta engagement at no charge (no price anchor set) in exchange for a testimonial and a written case study.
- Never publish the Assessment price with a "from" or "starting at." Fixed price signals confidence. Variable pricing signals negotiation, which signals uncertainty.

## Toolkit Section 10: The Governance Agent (Deferred)

The governance agent MVP is deferred to Phase 1 (post-first-revenue). Building a bot-driven diagnostic before the practice has paying clients is the build-instead-of-outreach failure mode in HD. When the agent is built, the following specifications apply.

### 10.1 What the Agent Must Do

- Collect organizational context (industry, employee count, jurisdictions, AI tool inventory, existing documentation, triggering question)
- Map jurisdiction to applicable regulations (Utah, Texas, Illinois, Colorado, EU)
- Walk organization through risk tier classification
- Generate governance policy sections reflecting specific context (not fill-in-the-blank templates)
- Run gap analysis against chosen framework (NIST RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act)
- Explain governance concepts in plain language

### 10.2 What the Agent Must NOT Do

- Generate full compliance opinions for specific regulations
- Provide legal advice or claim to substitute for legal review
- Generate incident response plans (too context-specific for v1)
- Perform bias audits or technical AI assessments
- Advise on AI system design, model selection, technical architecture

### 10.3 Design Safeguards

- Every generated document includes an explicit completeness statement listing what is and isn't covered
- Every regulatory claim has a verifiable citation
- Every output includes the non-buried disclaimer about legal advice
- Agent is scoped to governance only; technical questions redirect
- Knowledge freshness flag on all regulatory content with "last verified" date
- Human review gate on any regulatory content update

### 10.4 V1 Expansion Criteria

V2 unlocks only after: at least 50 organizations have completed v1 intake, output quality validated against real client situations, legal review has confirmed scope and disclaimer adequacy, regulatory update mechanism is operational.
