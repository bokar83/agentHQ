### Section 1: The Landscape


#### 1.1 The Governance Triangle

Three overlapping disciplines that clients conflate constantly. Clarity here signals expertise fast.

- **IT Governance:** The factory. Infrastructure, availability, cybersecurity, technology-to-strategy alignment. Keeps the systems running and safe from outside attack.
- **Data Governance:** The raw materials. Data quality, lineage, access controls, lifecycle. Ensures data is accurate and usable.
- **AI Governance:** The finished product. The unique behaviors and risks of algorithms. Ethics, bias, explainability, accountability, human oversight. Governs how decisions get made by non-human agents.

The core truth: you cannot have credible AI governance without reliable data governance. You cannot have reliable data governance without functional IT governance. They stack.


#### 1.2 The Six Core Principles in Practice

1. **Accountability:** Every deployed AI system has a named human owner.
2. **Transparency:** Users know when they are interacting with AI or AI-generated output.
3. **Fairness:** Models are tested for discriminatory bias before launch.
4. **Safety and Security:** Protection against adversarial attacks and against shadow AI (employees uploading sensitive data to unapproved tools).
5. **Explainability:** Constrained, rule-based AI workflows are preferred over black-box decision models for high-stakes actions.
6. **Human Oversight:** Workflows are designed so AI drafts, humans approve.


#### 1.3 The Legal and Framework Map (verified April 2026)

**Utah SB 149 AI Policy Act.** Effective May 1, 2024. The first US state law regulating private-sector use of generative AI. Applies to any individual or entity using generative AI to interact with people in Utah, regardless of where the organization is located. Administrative fines up to $2,500 per violation. This is the home-state authority play. Boubacar lives in Utah. The Utah AI Act has been in effect for nearly two years. Most Utah SMBs have not yet formally addressed it.

**Texas TRAIGA (HB 149).** Effective January 1, 2026. Intent-based liability framework for AI misuse. Exclusive enforcement by the Texas Attorney General. Civil penalties up to $200,000 per violation with a 60-day cure period. Applies to any entity conducting business in Texas or offering products to Texas residents.

**Illinois HB 3773.** Effective January 1, 2026. Amends the Illinois Human Rights Act. Prohibits employers from using AI in ways that discriminate against protected classes. Requires employer disclosure when AI is used in recruitment, hiring, promotion, discipline, or termination decisions.

**Colorado SB 24-205.** Effective June 30, 2026 (delayed from February 1, 2026 via SB 25B-004). Requires developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to use reasonable care to protect against algorithmic discrimination. Subject to active political revision; a repeal-and-replace bill was under consideration in early 2026. Monitor state legislative action through mid-2026.

> **WARNING:** Federal preemption risk is real. A December 11, 2025 executive order directed the DOJ to challenge state AI laws. The legal landscape remains unstable. Anchor messaging to operating-environment pressures (vendor questionnaires, client due diligence, insurance carrier requirements) rather than to specific regulatory deadlines that may move.

**EU AI Act.** In force since August 1, 2024. Banned practices enforceable since February 2, 2025. GPAI obligations since August 2, 2025. High-risk system requirements scheduled for August 2, 2026, though AI Omnibus negotiations may push this to December 2027 or August 2028. Fines up to EUR 35M or 7% of worldwide annual turnover for prohibited practices; up to EUR 15M or 3% for high-risk violations.

**NIST AI RMF.** US federal voluntary guidance. Four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. Voluntary is a legal status, not a recommendation to skip it. Increasingly referenced in litigation, procurement due diligence, and regulatory interpretation.

**ISO/IEC 42001.** International certifiable AI management system standard. Published 2023. Emerging as the certification version of AI governance. Catalyst Works does not claim 42001 expertise in year one; positions 42001 as a roadmap destination for Tier 3 clients.


#### 1.4 How SMBs Actually Implement Governance (and Fail To)

The dominant pattern in enterprise: top-down organizationally (leadership sets the Acceptable Use Policy) plus bottom-up architecturally (engineering implements an AI Gateway as a secure proxy).

For SMB owner-operators with 5 to 200 employees, the AI Gateway implementation is out of reach. No dedicated IT. No engineering team. The top-down organizational lever is the only lever available, and it has to work without technical enforcement. This is the core design constraint for any SMB governance program.

**Common failure modes:**

1. **Wait-and-see paralysis.** Banning AI outright leads employees to use unsanctioned personal tools. Shadow AI is infinitely riskier than governed AI because it leaves no documentation trail, no training opportunity, no incident response path.
2. **Vendor blindspot.** Owners vet the primary LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude) while ignoring that their CRM, HR platform, accounting software, and project management tools have silently added AI features trained on customer data.
3. **Template theater.** Generic AI policies downloaded from the internet. Signed once, never read again. Worse than no policy because it creates a paper record of compliance that fails under scrutiny.
4. **Decision-owner ambiguity.** No one is named accountable. When something goes wrong, the response is reactive and political rather than procedural.


#### 1.5 The Anatomy of a Working SMB AI Policy

Seven sections, each concrete enough that an employee can use it without asking a question:

1. **Purpose and Scope:** Why the policy exists and who it applies to.
2. **Risk Classification System:** Low / Medium / High / Prohibited, with one-sentence rules for each tier.
3. **Acceptable Use Rules:** What employees can use AI for, what they cannot.
4. **Data Handling and Privacy:** What data is explicitly prohibited from entering AI tools (client PII, financial data, protected health information, contract terms).
5. **Vendor Procurement Addendums:** The three clauses every AI vendor contract must include or disclose.
6. **Human Oversight Matrix:** What decisions require human review before AI output is delivered.
7. **Incident Response Protocol:** What happens when AI produces an error that affects a client.

Review triggers, not review calendars. The policy gets refreshed when a new regulation takes effect, a new AI vendor category is adopted, or an incident occurs.


### Section 2: The SHIELD Methodology

SHIELD is the Catalyst Works-owned methodology for AI Risk and Readiness Assessment. Six steps. Delivered in 14 days. $3,500 fixed.

- **S (Surface):** Complete AI tool inventory. Every tool touching the business, including embedded AI in software already in use.
- **H (Halt):** Identify the highest-risk tools and the highest-risk uses. Pause where necessary. Document what is happening in the meantime.
- **I (Inventory):** Formal register with risk tier, accountable owner, data types, vendor terms summary.
- **E (Educate):** Employee communication that translates the policy into plain-language rules the team can actually follow.
- **L (Lock):** Written Acceptable Use Policy and vendor approval process, customized to the client's industry and jurisdiction.
- **D (Document):** Final deliverable with Governance Maturity Briefing and Margin and Operational Risk Indicators section.

The SHIELD deliverable is the foot-in-the-door product. The Margin and Operational Risk Indicators section is the bridge to every follow-on engagement.

### Toolkit Section 1: The Discovery Session


#### 1.1 Session Architecture

The Signal Session ($497 productized offer) is **90 minutes.** The SHIELD Assessment kickoff diagnostic (internal first session of a paid engagement) is **90 to 120 minutes** (TBD). These are two different contexts.

| --- | ---: | --- |

| Current State | 5 to 20 min | Diagnostic questions: AI usage inventory, risk exposure, existing policies. Listen more than you speak. Take visible notes. Surface the gap between what they think they have and what they actually have. |
| Accountability Map | 20 to 30 min | Identify who owns what. Who is responsible for AI decisions today? Who gets called if something goes wrong? What happens when an employee uses an unapproved tool? This is where most SMBs discover they have no answers. |

#### 1.4 Diagnostic Questions by Category

These are the exact questions. Do not ask all of them. Use the first one in each category to gauge depth, then follow the thread. Your job is to surface the gap, not exhaust the topic. Starred questions (*) are the 11 high-signal questions used in the Signal Session (90-minute version).

**CATEGORY A: Current AI Usage**
- A1: Walk me through every AI tool your team uses on a regular basis, including tools built into software you already use. (*)
- A2: Are there tools your employees are using informally, outside of anything you have officially approved? (*)
- A3: Which of these tools touches client data, employee data, or proprietary business information? (*)
- A4: Who made the decision to adopt each of these tools, and what was the approval process?
- A5: Is there an AI system that is making or significantly influencing decisions that used to be made by a human?

**CATEGORY B: Risk Exposure**
- B1: If one of your AI tools gave a client wrong information or made a discriminatory decision, what would happen? Who would handle it? (*)
- B2: Have you had any situation where an AI tool produced an output that surprised you, embarrassed you, or caused a problem?
- B3: If a client asked you today how you use AI and what data protections you have in place, what would you say? (*)
- B4: Are any of your clients in regulated industries? Do you know if your AI use creates compliance obligations for them?
- B5: What would a data breach involving an AI vendor cost you financially, reputationally, in client relationships?

**CATEGORY C: Existing Policies**
- C1: Do you have a written policy that governs how your team uses AI? If yes, where does it live and when was it last updated? (*)
- C2: Does your employee handbook say anything about AI use?
- C3: What data are employees explicitly prohibited from inputting into AI tools?
- C4: If I asked one of your employees tomorrow what they are and are not allowed to do with AI, what would they say?
- C5: Have you reviewed your client contracts recently to check whether your AI use is consistent with your data handling obligations?

**CATEGORY D: Stakeholder Accountability**
- D1: If something goes wrong with an AI system tomorrow, who is the first call? Who owns the response? (*)
- D2: Who in your organization has authority to approve or reject the adoption of a new AI tool?
- D3: Does your leadership team have a shared understanding of what your AI risk exposure is?
- D4: Has AI governance ever come up in a board meeting, an investor conversation, or a client due diligence process?
- D5: If a regulator or auditor asked you to demonstrate responsible AI use, could you produce evidence within 48 hours?

**CATEGORY E: Vendor Relationships**
- E1: Have you read the AI-specific data provisions in your vendor contracts? Does your vendor use your data to train their models? (*)
- E2: Do you have a list of AI vendors your team is approved to use? Who maintains it?
- E3: When you evaluate a new AI software vendor, what specifically do you ask about their AI practices? (*)
- E4: If a vendor's AI system failed or was compromised, what notification would you receive and how quickly?
- E5: Do you have any contractual protections (indemnification, limitation of liability) covering AI-related vendor failures?

### Toolkit Section 2: Objection Handling

Do not rehearse these as scripts. Internalize the logic. The goal is genuine persuasion, not clever comebacks. Each response reframes the objection without dismissing it. Say each one aloud five times until natural.

**"We're too small to need this."**
Size determines the complexity of governance, not whether you need it. A three-person firm with one AI tool touching client data is exposed. The scale of the governance matches the scale of the operation, which is exactly what I help design.

**"We don't really use AI that much."**
Does your CRM have AI features? Does your email platform? Your accounting software? AI is embedded in most business software now, and you may be using it without knowing it. The question is not whether you use AI. It's whether you know how it's using your data.

**"We already have a privacy policy."**
A privacy policy covers how you handle data you collect. It does not cover how your employees use AI tools, what data they put into those tools, how your vendors use that data, or who is accountable when an AI system makes a wrong call. Those are four separate governance gaps that a privacy policy does not touch.

**"This feels like compliance overhead."**
Here's the alternative: one of your employees puts a client contract into an AI tool whose vendor uses it to train their model. Your client finds out. You have no policy, no documentation, no accountability structure. The conversation you have then costs more than governance would have. This is not overhead. It's insurance that you pay once and use forever.

**"We'll deal with this when regulations require it."**
Texas TRAIGA went live January 1, 2026. Illinois HB 3773 went live the same day. Colorado's AI Act takes effect June 30, 2026. Utah's AI Policy Act has been live since May 1, 2024. If you have employees or clients in those states, it's already required. And the organizations that wait for a regulatory trigger always pay more to remediate than they would have spent to build it right the first time.

**"We don't have budget for this right now."**
What's your legal bill if one of your vendors has an AI data incident and you're named in the client complaint? What does it cost you if a prospective client asks about your AI practices in a due diligence call and you have no answer? Governance is not a discretionary expense. It's a hedge against a specific class of operational and legal risk.

**"Can't I just use a template I find online?"**
You can. The same way you can use a contract template you find online. The risk is that a generic template does not reflect your specific AI tool stack, your actual data practices, your real vendor relationships, or the laws that apply to your specific jurisdiction and industry. A governance policy that does not reflect your actual operations is not just useless. It's actively misleading if it ever gets scrutinized.


### Toolkit Section 3: The Business Case for Skeptical Owner-Operators

Anchor every conversation to outcomes the client already cares about. Do not lead with compliance. Lead with what keeps them awake.

- **Liability:** If an AI tool makes a wrong decision and you have no governance documentation, you cannot demonstrate that you acted reasonably. Governance creates the paper trail of responsible practice.
- **Operational Continuity:** What happens when the AI tool your team depends on changes its pricing, gets acquired, or changes its data practices? A governance program includes vendor risk management and exit strategy.
- **Vendor Risk:** Most SMBs have signed AI vendor contracts without reading the AI-specific clauses. Some vendors explicitly reserve the right to use your data to train their models.
- **Employee Behavior:** Your employees are making decisions today about what data they put into AI tools. Without a written policy, those decisions are ungoverned. Governance gives them a framework so they solve problems in ways that do not create new ones.
- **Competitive Positioning:** Your enterprise clients and prospects are starting to ask about AI governance in due diligence. If you cannot answer "What is your AI governance policy?" you are at a disadvantage against competitors who can.

### Toolkit Section 6: Designing Governance That Stays Relevant

The failure mode is a policy written in 2026 that's still version 1.0 in 2028 while the organization has adopted twelve new AI tools. These mechanisms prevent that:

- **Amendment triggers baked into the policy text:** Specific events that automatically trigger review. New regulation. New AI vendor category. AI incident. Organizational restructure.
- **AI inventory review as a quarterly operations item:** Tie it to an existing meeting (quarterly business review, operations review) so it does not depend on someone remembering.
- **Tool adoption as review trigger:** Every time the organization adopts a new AI tool, the inventory and risk classification is the gate. No inventory update, no tool approval.
- **Sunset clauses on vendor assessments:** Assessments expire after 12 months and must be renewed.
- **Living document format:** Policies stored in version-controlled documents (Notion, Google Docs with version history). PDFs are archives. Living documents are systems.
- **Regulatory monitoring:** A named person responsible for monitoring AI regulatory developments. Can be a Google Alert. Must be someone's job.


### Toolkit Section 7: Content Angles and Educational Arc


#### 7.1 The 12 Misunderstood AI Governance Concepts

Each one is a LinkedIn post, an X thread, or a Signal Session talking point. Publish one per week starting in week three. Do not publish before the first beta session is delivered.

1. "AI governance" and "AI ethics" are not the same. Ethics is the values. Governance is the system that operationalizes values inside an organization.
2. Having a privacy policy does not mean you have AI governance. A privacy policy governs how you handle data you collect from users. It does not govern how your employees use AI tools.
3. "Low risk" AI is not "no risk" AI. Risk classification must account for scale and breadth of use, not just individual system risk level.
4. NIST AI RMF is voluntary, not optional. Voluntary means no penalty for non-adoption. It does not mean no consequences.
5. "The vendor handles it" is not a governance answer. Vendors handle their own data practices. They do not govern how your employees use the tool or who is accountable inside your organization.
6. Governance is not a one-time project. AI capabilities change quarterly. Regulations evolve monthly. A governance document written once and never reviewed is outdated by the time the ink dries.
7. "We only use AI for internal things" is not a defense. Internal AI use on customer data is the most common path to a compliance violation.
8. Shadow AI is not a technology problem. It is a process problem. Employees use unauthorized tools because the authorized tools are slower or less capable.
9. AI governance and AI adoption are not in conflict. Governed organizations adopt AI faster because they have cleared the risk runway in advance.
10. A "no AI" policy is not governance. It is a delay tactic. Employees will use AI anyway and the organization will have no visibility.
11. The biggest AI governance risk for most SMBs is not a major incident. It is accumulated small exposures that compound over 12 to 24 months.
12. Governance is not a one-time project. Regulations change. Tools change. Your team changes. A governance program is a cadence, not a deliverable.

### Toolkit Section 9: The Governance Agent (Deferred)

The governance agent MVP is deferred to Phase 1 (post-first-revenue). Building a bot-driven diagnostic before the practice has paying clients is the build-instead-of-outreach failure mode. Unlock: $12,000 cumulative Assessment revenue collected.

**When the agent is built, it must:**
- Collect organizational context (industry, employee count, jurisdictions, AI tool inventory, existing documentation, triggering question)
- Map jurisdiction to applicable regulations
- Walk organization through risk tier classification
- Generate governance policy sections reflecting specific context (not fill-in-the-blank templates)
- Run gap analysis against chosen framework (NIST RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act)
- Explain governance concepts in plain language

**The agent must NOT:**
- Generate full compliance opinions for specific regulations
- Provide legal advice or claim to substitute for legal review
- Perform bias audits or technical AI assessments
- Advise on AI system design, model selection, or technical architecture

**Every agent output must include:** "This output is a starting point for governance documentation, not legal advice. Any policy affecting employee rights, client data, or regulatory compliance should be reviewed by qualified legal counsel before implementation." Plus a "last verified" date on all regulatory content.
