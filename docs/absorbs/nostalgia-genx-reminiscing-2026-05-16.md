## 3. Post seeds (12, ship-ready in 7 days)

### Post 1 — LinkedIn (1,400 char)
**Hook:** "The transistor radio in my father's hand, Conakry, 1984"

Some of my earliest memories are my father, transistor radio pressed to his ear, BBC Africa crackling through the static. I thought he was listening for news. He was. But he was also listening for himself. For who he had been before he became the man holding three jobs to feed seven kids.

I am 47 now. I run an AI company in the US. I just realized I do the exact same thing with my own quiet moments, just with different signals.

We do not reminisce because we are old. We reminisce because we are tired and the past is the only place the math still adds up.

Gen X friends, raise your hand if you have caught yourself this week.

**Engagement driver:** Cohort recognition + African specificity others cannot copy.

### Post 2 — X thread (4 tweets)
1/ Gen X is 28% of TikTok now. We did not go for the dances. We went because something is happening in our chests and we do not have language for it yet.

2/ The retirement math says we are $400K short on average. The therapy says reminiscing is how the body asks for replanning.

3/ Most of us are doing the first part and skipping the second. Scrolling through 1985 instead of writing down what we want 2035 to look like.

4/ I did the swap this month. Pulled one childhood memory a day. Wrote one sentence about the version of me that memory built. Then planned one move forward. Try it for 7 days.

**Engagement driver:** Stat-anchored + actionable + cohort callout.

### Post 3 — LinkedIn (1,300)
**Hook:** "The first time I touched the internet"

Cyber cafe, Conakry, 1998. The keyboard had 4 keys missing. The screen took 90 seconds to load Hotmail. I remember the smell, the noise of the modem, the price (the equivalent of a meal). I remember thinking, this changes everything.

And it did, but not the way I expected. The thing the early internet promised was access. The thing it delivered was distraction.

Gen X is the only generation that remembers both versions: the one we were promised and the one we got. That memory is a competitive advantage, not a wound. We can see when AI is making the same trade. We have done this before.

### Post 4 — X thread (5 tweets)
1/ My mother had a saying: the one who does not look back forgets the road. Pular wisdom. I thought she meant be grateful.

2/ I am 47 now and I think she meant something else. She meant: your past is a map. If you do not study it, you will walk the same wrong turn twice.

3/ Gen X is reminiscing on TikTok and Pinterest and at kitchen tables right now. We are not stuck. We are studying the map.

4/ The question is not should we look back. The question is what are we going to do with what we see when we do.

5/ I am running a study-the-map week here. Drop one childhood moment that shaped how you work today. I will reply to every one.

### Post 5 — LinkedIn (1,200)
**Hook:** "I failed three businesses before this one worked"

Tile installation. Carpet cleaning sales. A consulting practice I shut down twice. Each one taught me something I did not want to learn. Each one feels like a different country I lived in.

I do not romanticize them. They were hard, and I was broke, and my wife was patient longer than she should have been. But here is the strange thing: the AI company I run now uses ideas from all three. The tile work taught me unit economics. The carpet cleaning taught me zero-risk pricing. The failed consulting taught me what I refuse to do this time.

Reminiscing is not pining for the past. It is auditing the warehouse.

### Post 6 — X thread (3)
1/ I made my first mixtape in 1989. Bambino, Mory Kante, a smuggled Whitney Houston track. I sequenced it for 40 minutes. I labeled the sides.

2/ That was the original recommendation engine. One human, with taste, picking songs for one other human.

3/ Spotify has 600 million users and still cannot do what a 12-year-old with a double cassette deck could do for a friend. Gen X has not forgotten this. Some of us are about to remember it loud.

### Post 7 — LinkedIn (1,400)
**Hook:** "My HR career was 14 years of watching people be afraid for the wrong reasons"

I spent 14 years in corporate HR. I watched layoffs handled badly, severance miscalculated, COBRA misexplained, and grown men cry in conference rooms because they did not know what they did not know.

The fear I saw was almost never about the layoff itself. It was about not having a story for what came next. Gen X is heading into a period where this will matter more than any time in our working lives.

The thing I learned in HR that I am taking everywhere now: the people who land on their feet are not the most senior or the most credentialed. They are the ones who already had a second story written down before they needed it.

Write the second story before you need it.

### Post 8 — X thread (4)
1/ Saturday mornings in our house in Conakry, the TV stayed off until chores were done. When it finally came on, I watched cartoons in 3 languages: French dubs, Arabic dubs, the rare English original.

2/ I did not know I was building the brain that would later let me work across cultures, switch register on a call, read a room in any language.

3/ The cartoons were not the lesson. The translation between them was.

4/ Gen X parents, your kids are building these private languages right now and you do not see them yet. Let them.

### Post 9 — X (single 280)
If you are 45 to 60 and reading old yearbooks lately, you are not lost. You are looking for the launch codes. Pull the moments where you were brave when nothing was guaranteed. That version of you knew something. The current version forgot. Reminisce, then replan.

### Post 10 — LinkedIn (1,500)
**Hook:** "Three songs I cannot listen to without crying, and what each one taught me"

I am 47, and there are exactly three songs I cannot get through without my throat tightening.

Mory Kante, Yeke Yeke. The first wedding I attended, age 6, in Conakry. I learned that joy is loud and communal and that adults dance differently when they think children are not watching.

Tracy Chapman, Fast Car. A bus ride in Atlanta, 2001, broke and 22 and convinced America had lied to me. I learned that the people who write the truest songs are also broke and that this is not an accident.

Asa, Jailer. The first time I held my first daughter. I learned that I was about to spend the rest of my life being afraid for someone else.

These are not memories. They are the audit log of who I have been.

Gen X friends, drop one song. Tell me what it taught you.

### Post 11 — LinkedIn (1,200)
**Hook:** "The phone book is the thing I miss the most and the thing I should miss the least"

We had a phone book on the kitchen counter. Heavy, yellow, slightly damp from rain. I called my friends by dialing 7 digits and asking their mother if Mamadou was home.

The phone book is gone and I miss it. But here is the truth: I do not miss the phone book. I miss the version of me who had the patience to dial 7 digits and the courage to talk to somebody's mother.

Reminiscing is not about the object. It is about the muscle the object built. AI is going to take most of those muscles soon. The question is which ones we choose to keep using on purpose.

### Post 12 — LinkedIn (1,400)
**Hook:** "I am running a 7-day experiment, and I am inviting Gen X in"

For the next 7 days I am pulling one childhood memory per day, naming the trait it built, and writing one specific move forward that uses that trait.

I am calling it Reminisce + Replan. It is free. There is nothing to buy at the end.

I am doing it because (a) Gen X is the loneliest generation online right now, (b) the retirement math says we are $400K short on average, and (c) reminiscing without a plan is just doomscrolling with better lighting.

If you want to do it with me, drop a comment. I will share my prompt for day one tomorrow.

We are not stuck in the past. We are using it for fuel.

**Voice notes:** no em-dashes, no signature line, first-name only, Guinea/Conakry specific, no Senegal/Dakar.

## 6. AdamDelDuca 7 anti-pattern self-check

| Anti-pattern | Risk | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Advertiser-flight | LOW | "Transition" / "reminisce" / "reset" framing is brand-safe. Skip "fired" / "unemployed" / "depression" in titles. |
| 2. Voice-creep | HIGH (mitigated) | Reminiscence content is high-template (everyone is doing it). African-Guinean-Pular-HR specificity is the differentiator. CTQ must check: does each post carry a detail that NO ONE else in the cohort can write? If 2+ generic posts in a row, pause. |
| 3. Scope-creep | MEDIUM | Cap: 3 LI posts/week + 1 X thread/week + 1 newsletter mention/month from this lane. Faceless Soft Landing runs independently. |
| 4. Audience-mismatch | LOW | Gen X already in Boubacar's audience (LinkedIn skews 35-55). Crossover with existing CW + 1stGen ICP is high. |
| 5. Saturation / template-fatigue | MEDIUM-HIGH | Reminisce + Replan reframe is the moat. Pure-nostalgia drift = kill signal. |
| 6. Monetization timing | LOW | Lead magnet free; T1 product 30 days after first post; T3 product 60 days. No immediate hard sell. |
| 7. Brand-spine contamination | MEDIUM | Personal LinkedIn must not become a memoir account. Mix: 60% AI + business, 20% reminisce + replan, 20% other. Tripwire: reminisce posts cross 35% of monthly output = pause 2 weeks. |
