01What I looked at

Headline, your latest post, your About. The headline gets a surgical fix. The post game is where the real opportunity lives. About reads as a resume right now, and that is a separate conversation for another day.

Note on formatting: bolds in this audit are for your eye, to mark what I am drawing attention to. Do not copy them into LinkedIn (LinkedIn does not render rich formatting anyway). Plain text only when you paste.

02The headline (surgical fix)

Your current headline
Commercial Business Manager at TruGreen

This is what LinkedIn auto-populates the moment you accept a new role. Most people leave it. It does one job: it tells the reader where you work. That is the smallest job a headline can do.

One job title. Zero buyers.

Replace with
I help commercial property managers stop chasing their lawn care vendor and start trusting them.

Why this version works. "Commercial property managers" names the buyer in three words. "Stop chasing their lawn care vendor and start trusting them" names the actual pain (vendor that disappears after month four) and the actual outcome (a person you can rely on). No "excellence", no "solutions", no "dynamic". Plain language matches the way you sell, which is by being the human who shows up.

03The bigger play: get your voice on the page

You have one post from three weeks ago. The line in it is good: "I love the look of fresh mow lines but not fresh, bad fertilizer application lines." Specific. Visual. The kind of observation only someone who has stood on a hundred properties would notice. It earns the second line.

Twenty years of knowing you, I have always thought you have a voice that should be heard online. That mow-lines hook is the proof. You wrote one. You can write fifty.

Cadence

Once or twice a week. Not daily. Pick two fixed days (Tuesday + Friday morning works, or whatever fits your route schedule), and protect them. Twelve posts in three months puts you on the radar of every commercial property manager in your network. Twenty-four puts you on their inbound shortlist. The cadence matters more than the polish at this stage.

What to post about (4 lanes)

  1. Industry observation — the mow-lines lane. What you noticed on a property this week that ninety percent of people would miss.
  2. Common buyer pain — the thing eight out of ten commercial property managers complain about when you first meet them.
  3. Counter-intuitive truth — the thing most buyers believe about lawn care that is actually wrong.
  4. Behind-the-scenes — what actually goes into a great fertilizer application or seasonal program that no one outside the industry sees.

Stay in these four lanes. Skip the motivational quote, the holiday post, the "happy to announce" template. None of those sound like you.

Three example hooks (line 1 only, you write the rest)

Lane 1 · Industry observation
"The diagonal brown stripe across the field tells me everything. Someone applied fertilizer with the spreader on the wrong setting. Three weeks later, the property manager will call me asking what happened."
Lane 2 · Common buyer pain
"Every commercial property manager I have met thinks the lawn care problem is price. After twenty-five years of doing this, the real problem is the vendor who shows up the first month and stops returning calls by month four."
Lane 3 · Behind-the-scenes
"What separates a good fertilizer application from a great one is not the chemical mix. It is whether the technician walked the property the week before, noticed the bare spots near the loading dock, and adjusted the route."

These are openings, not full posts. You take it from there. Your voice, your stories, your experience on actual properties. The hook earns the click. The story earns the second read. The pattern repeated weekly earns the inbound.

Write one post this week in any of the four lanes. Pick the lane that feels easiest to write right now and just go. ~15 min.
