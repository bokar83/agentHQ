## Premise 1: "I want this done such that I gain whether the market goes up or down."

**Verdict: FAILS as stated. Survives in narrow form.**

The wheel strategy (cash-secured puts + covered calls) is directionally biased long.
The mechanics are:

- Cash-secured put: you collect premium and agree to buy 100 shares at strike if assigned.
  Your profit zone is: stock stays at or above strike at expiration. Your loss zone is: stock
  drops below your cost basis (strike minus premium collected).

- Covered call: you own shares and sell the right to buy them from you at a higher price.
  Your profit zone is: stock stays flat or rises slowly. Your loss zone is: stock drops
  below your cost basis in the shares.

Neither leg profits in a sustained downtrend. In a sharp drawdown, the put seller absorbs
the full loss below the cost basis with no hedge. The premium collected is a cushion, not
a hedge.

**What the historical indices show:**

The CBOE S&P 500 PutWrite Index (PUT), which systematically sells one-month at-the-money
puts on the S&P 500, has these characteristics (CBOE published data, 1986-2023):

- Long-run annualized return: approximately 9-10%, comparable to the S&P 500 total return.
- Sharpe ratio: approximately 0.60-0.65, higher than buy-and-hold (approximately 0.45)
  because volatility is lower in steady markets.
- Q4 2008 drawdown: PUT index fell approximately 26-28%. Not catastrophic, but not neutral.
  The premium collected cushioned the S&P's 38% fall in 2008, but it did not prevent loss.
- March 2020: PUT index fell approximately 22% peak-to-trough before recovering.

The CBOE BXM (buy-write, covered calls on S&P 500): similar profile, approximately
8-9% annualized, lower drawdowns than pure equity, but still directionally long.

Source: CBOE Benchmark Indexes (cboe.com/products/benchmarks), published monthly.
Ibbotson (2012) "New Thinking About Portfolio Rebalancing" uses PUT/BXM extensively.
Whaley (2002) "Return and Risk of CBOE Buy Write Monthly Index" in Journal of Derivatives.

**The truthful version of Premise 1:**

The wheel earns above-average risk-adjusted returns in sideways markets and mild uptrends.
In sharp crashes, it loses less than pure equity but still loses. In a prolonged bear market
(S&P down 30%+ sustained), the wheel strategy underperforms cash. The premium is income
in exchange for absorbing downside risk, not a tool for omnidirectional profit.

## Premise 2: "The market will dip soon so I need to be ready."

**Verdict: FAILS as a timing signal. Produces correct preparation argument only.**

Retail investors predicting near-term market direction are systematically wrong:

- DALBAR QAIB (Quantitative Analysis of Investor Behavior) reports annually. The 2022 edition
  covering 30 years shows the average equity fund investor earned approximately 6.0% annually
  versus the S&P 500's approximately 10.6% annualized. The gap is almost entirely timing
  decisions: buying after rallies, selling after drops.

- Odean (1999) "Do Investors Trade Too Much?" (American Economic Review): retail investors'
  trades predict returns negatively. The stocks they buy underperform; the stocks they sell
  outperform.

- Welch and Goyal (2008) "A Comprehensive Look at the Empirical Performance of Equity Premium
  Prediction" (Review of Financial Studies): virtually every market-timing predictor fails
  out-of-sample. Macro signals and valuation ratios have near-zero predictive power over
  1-12 month horizons.

- Graham and Harvey (1996, 2001) surveys of CFO market expectations: CFO 12-month S&P 500
  forecasts have a negative correlation with actual outcomes. Expert consensus is no better
  than random.

The feeling that "the market will dip soon" is structurally always available to a human brain.
Markets have declined in approximately 30% of calendar months since 1950. The brain
pattern-matches recent volatility into this prediction. This is not insight; it is the base rate
dressed as analysis.

**What survives from Premise 2:**

The preparation argument is valid regardless of timing prediction. If capital is sitting idle
in a savings account at 4-5% APY, moving some toward a structured put-selling strategy that
targets 10-15% annualized is a directional improvement on a risk-adjusted basis over a long
horizon. The timing prediction is unnecessary and probably harmful. The preparation case
stands on its own.

## Premise 3: "I could have made money on GEV at $150, $200, then below $200. Now above $1K."

**Verdict: Survivorship bias + anchoring. Fails as a signal for current strategy.**

GE Vernova (GEV) is a real company. Its trajectory: spun off from GE in April 2024 at
approximately $150-160. It ran to above $400 by late 2024, pulled back, and has since risen
substantially (the reference to "above $1K" likely reflects 2025-2026 price action in a high
energy/power infrastructure cycle).

Three cognitive errors are embedded in this premise:

**Error 1 — Survivorship bias.** For every GEV-style run, hundreds of spinoffs, industrial
names, and "obvious" thesis stocks went nowhere or declined. The stocks that ran are
memorable. The ones that didn't are not recalled. This is not a systematic edge; it is
post-hoc pattern-matching on a winner.

**Error 2 — Hindsight precision.** "$150, $200, then below $200" suggests three distinct
entry points were identifiable in real time. They were not. The decision at each point
required holding through uncertainty, drawdowns, and news events that looked bad at the
time. The narrative is constructed backward from the outcome.

**Error 3 — Wheel-strategy mismatch.** The wheel strategy on GEV at current prices (if
above $1K per share) requires $100,000+ in cash to sell a single cash-secured put contract
(100 shares times $1,000+ strike). This is not a Tier 1 or Tier 2 candidate for a $10-50K
wheel account. The premise points toward a stock that is now priced out of range for the
stated capital constraints.

**What survives from Premise 3:**

The energy/infrastructure/industrial thesis (GEV's sector) is a legitimate watchlist filter.
Companies with GEV's profile characteristics (newly spun off, misunderstood, boring
sector, strong free cash flow, no analyst coverage initially) are a valid screening category.
GEV itself is cited as a lesson in conviction, not as a current trade signal.

## Premise 4: "Opportunity cost of acting is high, but not acting is also a great risk."

**Verdict: Partially true but asymmetric framing that can rationalize premature action.**

The framing treats action and inaction as symmetric risks. They are not in this context:

- Acting too early with underpracticed mechanics on a wheel strategy can result in permanent
  capital loss (assigned stock at $40, stock drops to $20, forced to hold or take a realized
  loss of 50% on the position). This is a recoverable but costly mistake that takes months
  to exit cleanly.

- Not acting means the capital stays in a savings account or broad index. At 4-5% HYSA rates
  or 10% S&P 500 long-run average, this is not a "great risk." It is opportunity cost only if
  the alternative strategy materially outperforms, which requires skill acquisition first.

The real risk of inaction is: Boubacar builds no options trading skill during a period when
capital is available and time is limited. This is a valid concern. But the solution is
paper trading now, not live capital now. Paper trading costs zero and builds the exact skill
that converts the opportunity cost argument from theory to practice.

The "inaction is also risk" framing is structurally similar to a sales close technique:
"If you don't buy now, you'll miss out." It deserves scrutiny. The actual risk of inaction
for six months of paper trading is approximately zero compared to the risk of live capital
with premature skill.

**What survives from Premise 4:**

The urgency to start the learning process is legitimate. Starting paper trading this week
is both low-risk and high-value. The urgency to deploy live capital is not supported by
the same argument.
